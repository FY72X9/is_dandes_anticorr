\begin{abstract}
Village fund corruption in Indonesia represents a persistent governance failure: between
2015 and 2023, the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) documented over 600 Dana Desa
cases totalling more than IDR~433 billion. Despite accelerating advances in machine
learning (ML)-based financial fraud detection, no existing study has applied these methods
to the specific institutional and data conditions of Indonesian village fund governance.
This systematic literature review (SLR) maps and critically synthesises the existing
evidence base to identify what ML methods exist, what feature engineering frameworks have
been validated, and what structural gaps preclude direct application to Dana Desa anomaly
detection. Following PRISMA 2020 guidelines, the review screened 1,001 records and
included 45 papers (2018--2026). Thematic synthesis, bibliometric cluster analysis, and a
Design Science Research (DSR) framework matrix reveal four analytical themes: an
Operationalization Chasm (no study bridges ML detection with village governance), a
Scalability Illusion (performance claims conditioned on inapplicable assumptions), an
Absence of IS Theory (62\% of technical papers contain no IS-theoretical grounding), and
a Ground Truth Paradox (circular synthetic-data validation). Three critical research voids
directly motivate a primary empirical study designed as a Design Science Research artefact
to bridge the identified gaps.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

Village fund governance in Indonesia operates at a scale that makes it both critical and
vulnerable. The Village Law (UU No.~6/2014) channels fiscal allocations to over 74,000
village governments annually --- a programme exceeding IDR~70 trillion per fiscal year.
This fiscal decentralisation model places budget execution authority with village officials
who frequently operate outside the supervisory reach of formal audit institutions. The KPK
recorded 601 Dana Desa corruption cases between 2015 and 2023 \cite{method_kpk2023},
implicating village officials in embezzlement, fictitious project expenditure, and budget
absorption manipulation. The financial exposure is systemic, not episodic: recurrence
across consecutive fiscal years in the same villages suggests structural principal-agent
failure rather than isolated opportunistic behaviour \cite{method_jensen1976}.

Current governance responses rely primarily on ex-post audit cycles, which detect fraud
after disbursement has occurred. The capacity gap is structural: Inspektorat Daerah
(APIP, Regional Government Inspectorate) units audit a small fraction of villages
annually, and Badan Pemeriksa Keuangan (BPK, State Audit Agency) sampling is confined
to aggregate financial statements rather than activity-level expenditure records. This
leaves the operationally critical data layer --- Siskeudes, the village financial
management information system --- activity records largely unscrutinised in real time. ML-based financial fraud detection has accumulated
substantial evidence over the past decade, with Isolation Forest, Local Outlier Factor
(LOF), graph neural networks (GNNs), and autoencoder-based methods reporting AUC-ROC
values exceeding 0.90 on benchmark datasets \cite{p003,p007,p015,p022,p026,p041,p043}.
Yet this technical literature addresses fundamentally different problem conditions:
centralised commercial systems with labelled transaction data. Whether these methods adapt
to village governance conditions --- fragmented records, unlabelled data, sub-national
institutional variation --- is an open question no existing study addresses.

This SLR pursues three research questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1}: What ML-based anomaly detection methods exist in the financial domain
and what performance characteristics do they demonstrate across data and institutional
contexts?
\item \textbf{RQ2}: What feature engineering frameworks operationalize corruption as
machine-detectable computational signals, and how transferable are they to public-sector
governance?
\item \textbf{RQ3}: What structural gaps prevent application of ML detection methods to
decentralised village fund governance, and what design requirements must a primary study
address?
\end{itemize}

This review adopts an IS perspective that frames ML anomaly detection as an IS governance
intervention --- a designed artefact embedded within institutional processes to support
human decision-making \cite{method_hevner2004}. This framing carries a critical
implication: complete artefact evaluation must assess not only predictive accuracy but
also institutional deployability, interpretability, and theoretical alignment with
principal-agent corruption dynamics \cite{method_jensen1976}. The contribution of this
SLR is therefore not merely a gap catalogue --- it is a structured evidence base that
specifies what a viable primary study must design, how it must validate, and why the
two-tradition divide constitutes a theoretically tractable --- not merely empirical ---
problem.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{ML-Based Financial Fraud Detection}

Contemporary reviews document three dominant method families: ensemble tree-based
classifiers, deep learning architectures (LSTM, Autoencoder, Transformer), and
graph-based methods \cite{p001,p003,p004,p022,p037}. Unsupervised methods --- Isolation
Forest \cite{p015}, LOF \cite{p041}, and One-Class SVM --- have gained traction where
ground truth labels are unavailable \cite{p003,p015,p041}. The practical implication is
that label availability determines which method families are viable. In the Dana Desa
context, where confirmed fraud convictions exist in isolated KPK case files rather than
structured database labels, supervised methods \cite{p003,p007,p022,p026,p039}
are operationally inaccessible without prior unsupervised or semi-supervised labelling.

Existing SLRs evaluate performance on private-sector datasets and extrapolate
generalizability without establishing whether institutional and data assumptions hold
in public governance contexts \cite{p001,p022,p037}. This constitutes the Scalability
Illusion: evaluating methods under institutional conditions that bear no structural
resemblance to the target deployment context. Procurement fraud detection \cite{p008,p043}
represents the closest analogue to village fund expenditure anomalies, but addresses
centralised national procurement systems, not decentralised village-level disbursement.

\subsection{Village Fund Governance and Corruption Patterns}

Dana Desa governance literature documents corruption typologies grounded in agency
theory \cite{method_jensen1976}: budget absorption manipulation, fictitious procurement,
and administrative fraud \cite{method_kpk2023}. Digital governance studies \cite{p013,p016,p039,p044}
provide IS framing for technology-institution dynamics but contain no computational
detection methods. This reveals a structural problem: the community with the richest
understanding of corruption mechanisms (governance) has not operationalized that
understanding as computable features; the community with the strongest detection
algorithms (ML) has not applied them to governance contexts where fraud mechanisms
differ fundamentally from commercial transaction fraud.

\subsection{Theoretical Framework: DSR and IS Success}

This review employs the three-cycle DSR model \cite{method_hevner2004}. The DSR
framework matrix (Section~\ref{sec:results}) confirms the entire literature fails
the DESIGN$\times$village criterion.

The DeLone and McLean IS Success Model \cite{method_delone2003} provides artefact
assessment dimensions beyond AUC-ROC: information quality, system quality, and net
benefits. Agency Theory \cite{method_jensen1976} establishes that the principal's
information asymmetry --- not simply the agent's opportunism --- is the structural
variable a detection system must reduce.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% =====================================================================

This review follows PRISMA 2020 \cite{method_prisma2020} and adopts Thomas and Harden's
thematic synthesis \cite{method_thomas2008}. Complementary evidence derives from
bibliometric analysis \cite{method_donthu2021} and a DSR framework matrix
\cite{method_hevner2004}. Figure~\ref{fig:framework} shows the PRISMA 2020 screening
flow.

\subsection{Search Strategy and Eligibility}

Five databases were searched: OpenAlex, Scopus, IEEE Xplore, Web of Science, and
Semantic Scholar. Two string sets were applied: primary (ML methods + public finance
fraud) and supplementary (village fund governance). Initial retrieval yielded 1,001
unique records.

Inclusion criteria required: ML/AI methods for financial anomaly detection (IC-01);
public sector governance or corruption studies (IC-02); IS-theoretical frameworks
(IC-03); publication year 2010--2026 (IC-04); peer-reviewed format (IC-05); and English
or Indonesian language (IC-06). A domain-override protocol retained 12 borderline
papers (quality score 4.0--4.99) with direct Dana Desa relevance.

\subsection{Quality Assessment and Selection}

Each paper received a composite quality score (0--10) across five dimensions:
methodological rigour, theoretical grounding, contextual relevance, reproducibility,
and evidence strength. Journal SCImago quartile enrichment was applied to 194 records.
Inclusion threshold: $\geq 5.0$; domain-override threshold: $\geq 4.0$.

Inter-coder reliability reached $\kappa = 0.307$ initially; after adjudication,
$\kappa = 1.000$. Final included corpus: \textbf{N\,=\,45}
\cite{p001,p002,p003,p004,p005,p006,p007,p008,p009,p010,p011,p012,p013,p014,p015,
p016,p017,p018,p019,p020,p021,p022,p023,p024,p025,p026,p027,p028,p029,p030,
p031,p032,p033,p034,p035,p036,p037,p038,p039,p040,p041,p042,p043,p044,p045}.

\subsection{Synthesis Methods}

Thematic synthesis \cite{method_thomas2008} processed 613 code instances across 45
papers into 10 descriptive themes (DT1--DT10) and 4 analytical themes (AT1--AT4).
A DSR framework matrix \cite{method_hevner2004} mapped papers across three cycles
(DESIGN, RELEVANCE, RIGOR) and six governance levels. Bibliometric analysis
\cite{method_donthu2021} examined temporal trends and keyword co-occurrence.

\subsubsection{Coding Protocol and Quality Assessment}

Two researchers independently coded a 20\% random sample (n=9) to establish
inter-rater reliability ($\kappa = 0.307$ initially, $\kappa = 1.000$ after
refinement). Each paper received a composite quality score (0--10) across five
dimensions. A domain-override protocol retained 12 borderline papers (score 4.0--4.99)
with direct Dana Desa relevance.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity Analysis}

Three quality thresholds were tested: T1 ($\geq$4.0, N=45), T2 ($\geq$4.5, N=23),
T3 ($\geq$5.0, N=14) \cite{method_higgins2011}. T1 encompasses all domain-override
papers; T2 retains only substantive evidence; T3 applies the strictest filter.
This multi-threshold approach demonstrates finding stability.

\subsubsection{Bibliometric Network Analysis}

Keyword co-occurrence analysis used fractional counting with a >0.6 threshold.
Cross-cluster co-occurrence counts above 2 indicated bridging relations. Temporal
analysis examined publication patterns 2018--2026.

\subsection{Analytical Themes}

Cross-paper analysis (121 inter-paper relations) produced four analytical themes.

\textbf{AT1 --- The Operationalization Chasm}: The corpus bifurcates into two
non-communicating traditions: 23 papers develop ML methods without governance
engagement; 26 papers document village corruption without computational approaches.
Three bridging papers exist but none unite both traditions in a single study design.
Six silencing relations confirm the absence: no paper applies ML to Dana Desa data;
no paper proposes a village fund feature set; no paper maps corruption typology to
ML-detectable signals (Figure~\ref{fig:chasm}).

\textbf{AT2 --- The Scalability Illusion}: Technical papers report AUC-ROC $>$0.95
under assumptions that do not hold in village governance: centralised databases,
labelled data, and high transaction volume. Five papers validate on synthetic data,
creating circular validation that cannot support generalization claims.

\textbf{AT3 --- The Absence of IS Theory}: 28/45 papers (62\%) contain no IS-theoretical
framing. IS theory appears exclusively in the governance cluster, never co-occurring
with detection methods. No paper evaluates a detection artefact using the DeLone-McLean
IS Success Model \cite{method_delone2003} or any adoption framework.

\textbf{AT4 --- The Ground Truth Paradox}: Five papers report high performance on
synthetic or self-labelled data while acknowledging that real-world fraud labels are
unavailable --- a circular validation pattern that cannot support operational deployment.

\subsubsection{Coding Protocol and Inter-Rater Reliability}

The coding protocol was developed iteratively. Two researchers independently coded
a random sample of 20\% of papers (n=9) to establish inter-rater reliability.
Cohen's kappa was calculated for each code. Initial agreement was moderate
($\kappa = 0.307$), reflecting interpretive differences regarding code boundaries
and thematic granularity. Through iterative discussion and protocol refinement,
final agreement reached perfect consistency ($\kappa = 1.000$) for all codes.
This process enhanced code reliability and ensured consistent application across
the full corpus.

\subsubsection{Quality Assessment Framework}

Each paper received a composite quality score (0--10) across five dimensions:
methodological rigour (experimental design, statistical validity), theoretical
grounding (explicit theoretical framework), contextual relevance (public sector
applicability), reproducibility (data/code availability), and evidence strength
(effect sizes, confidence intervals). Journal SCImago quartile enrichment was
applied to 194 records to account for publication venue quality. A domain-override
protocol retained 12 borderline papers (quality score 4.0--4.99) with direct
Dana Desa relevance, documented before analysis began to ensure transparency.

T2 removes the weakest borderline papers, retaining only those with substantive

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Answering the Research Questions}

\textbf{RQ1}: Isolation Forest, LOF, and autoencoder methods suit unlabelled, low-volume
village data. Supervised methods require labels unavailable in the Dana Desa context.
GNNs need transaction-graph infrastructure absent from current records.

\textbf{RQ2}: Five transferable feature categories emerge: single-bidder ratios, amendment
frequencies, price escalation, timing concentration, and vendor diversity. Village-specific
additions include budget absorption anomalies and disbursement timing irregularities.
No prior study integrates these into a unified detection framework.

\textbf{RQ3}: Three CRITICAL gaps preclude direct application (G1--G3), confirmed through
thematic synthesis, bibliometric disconnection, and DSR empty cells. The Operationalization
Chasm reflects an epistemological difference neither tradition has bridged.

\subsection{Theoretical Contributions}

The ML field optimises for AUC-ROC, orthogonal to IS success dimensions (information
quality, system quality, net benefits) \cite{method_delone2003}. Without IS-theoretical
grounding, no framework exists for designing institutional workflows auditors require.

Neither tradition specifies corruption as a computational feature in village governance.
Bridging the chasm requires a corruption typology simultaneously sociologically valid
(grounded in KPK modus operandi) and computationally actionable.

\subsection{Implications}

\textbf{Research}: Future work must develop boundary-spanning frameworks translating
corruption mechanisms into computational features while preserving theoretical fidelity
to institutional dynamics. New evaluation paradigms are needed for label-scarce contexts.

\textbf{Practice}: For Indonesian anti-corruption agencies, this review provides a
structured evidence base to prioritise: (1) unsupervised ensemble approaches for
unlabelled data, (2) feature engineering grounded in KPK-documented modus operandi,
and (3) explainability mechanisms aligned with auditor workflows.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =====================================================================

This SLR synthesised 45 papers to map the evidence base for ML-based financial anomaly
detection and identify structural gaps that preclude application to Dana Desa village fund
governance. Four analytical themes characterise the literature: the Operationalization
Chasm (no study bridges ML detection with village governance data), the Scalability
Illusion (AUC-ROC performance conditioned on inapplicable assumptions), the Absence of
IS Theory (62\% of detection papers), and the Ground Truth Paradox (circular synthetic-data
validation). Five structured gaps --- three CRITICAL, one PARTIAL, one METHODOLOGICAL ---
form a dependency chain motivating a DSR-compliant primary study.

The DSR matrix delivers the structural centrepiece: DESIGN$\times$village\,=\,0 across
all 45 papers and all three sensitivity thresholds. The RELEVANCE (14 papers) and RIGOR
(11 papers) knowledge exists; the artefact connecting them does not. Addressing the three
CRITICAL gaps (G1--G3) constitutes the explicit design agenda for the subsequent primary
empirical study.

\textbf{Limitations}: absence of pre-registered protocol; potential underrepresentation of
Chinese-language GNN literature; DT5 quality scoring disadvantage for Indonesian journals.
\textbf{Future research}: (1) execute a primary DSR-compliant study applying an
unsupervised ensemble to real village fund disbursement data with IS Success Model
evaluation; (2) extend toward real-time monitoring integration with
SiKPA/SIMDA village financial systems; (3) replicate across provinces and comparable
international fiscal decentralisation programmes.
