\begin{abstract}
The Dana Desa mechanism annually disburses approximately Rp~71 trillion to 75,259 villages, whereas the detected frauds, totaling 640 instances amounting to Rp 598.13 billion of state loss, indicate that the detection mechanism is not working efficiently. Although there is data on all activities in the form of expenditures saved in Siskeudes, there is no automatic pre-inspection screening mechanism currently available at the provincial level. In this research, an unsupervised machine learning framework with Isolation Forest (IF), Local Outlier Factor (LOF), and Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA) models is utilized to analyze the 99,692 activity-level expenditure data obtained from jaga.id in fiscal years 2023-2025 from Jambi Province. Seven-typology features indicating the modus operandi are analyzed through the framework. Through a multi-paradigm consensus approach, where a consensus from at least two out of three methods is needed, 3,107 anomalous cases (3.1\%) were detected. The LOF model generated the best bimodality coefficient (BC = 0.957). Anomalies are found in two typologies, specifically Mark-up (T1: 50.6\%) and Cross-Category Dump (T7: 50.5\%), based on the court data. Ten villages examined show that six villages (47.1\%) are prioritized for further investigation. The application of the machine learning framework reduces the APIP examination task size from 99,692 to 642 cases (93.5\%).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}


Dana Desa in Indonesia is considered one of the biggest intergovernmental fiscal transfers to sub-national governments amounting to around Rp 71 trillion annually to 75,259 villages according to Law No. 6 of 2014 on Villages~\cite{ref1}. The total number of corruption court judgments regarding misallocation of village funds until 2024 was 591, involving 640 individuals who caused the state losses of Rp 598.13 billion~\cite{ref2}. Moreover, as reported by KPK, there were 851 incidents of village fund misallocation from 2015 to the present, out of which village heads committed more than 60\% of offenses~\cite{ref3}. Hence, it is obvious that the predominant measure of dealing with village funds' corruption is reactive, not proactive and data-driven.

Sistem Keuangan Desa (Siskeudes), hosted by BPKP, reports on village funds' expenditures in relation to their expenditure activities at each stage of the disbursement process: planning activity types, allocating budgets, expenditure realization per tranche (T1, T2, T3), and the choice of procurement. This information constitutes a rich database of administrative signals in which some patterns are encoded implicitly to distinguish corruption-free from suspicious expenditure activities. However, there are no systems implemented for systematically querying the database to find anomalies before the final cycle of inspection and closing of the window for disbursement.

Despite the implementation of Trisula preventive strategy by KPK~\cite{ref4} and four village-level tools (\textit{Desa Antikorupsi}, Siswaskeudes, MCP, jaga.id~\cite{ref5,ref6,ref7,ref8,ref9}), it turns out that none of those measures includes the step of screening activities for abnormalities during the year of disbursement as shown in Section~\ref{sec:kpk_prevention}.

Most of the related literature pays attention to the retrospection ~\cite{ref10}, governance and accountability framework ~\cite{ref11}, and triangle fraud diagnostic ~\cite{ref12}. Some machine learning techniques applicable in this case include supervised classification, which does not fit into the proposed problem as labels are absent~\cite{ref13,ref14}. Thus, the use of an unsupervised anomaly detection algorithm in the analysis of expenditure records of village funds in Jambi Province for fiscal years 2023-2025 will be required.


This paper uses the Jambi Province as an important empirical case study in terms of structural analysis. For the years 2024–2026, there are only eleven reports related to corruption in Jambi province, reported by jaga.id, which amount to 1.4\% of 761 complaints across Indonesia, as compared to the case of Sumatera Utara, which records eighty-one complaints ~\cite{ref9}. Such underreporting does not mean that there is not enough monitoring environment, but rather indicates that there is no exposure to corruption problem; four court cases of parallel investigations in Jambi province report that losses totaling Rp 2.301 billion occurred in Kerinci, Muaro Jambi, and Tanjung Jabung Timur districts with detection delay of two to five years ~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. There are three research questions formulated for this research: (RQ1) Which is the best feature construct in discriminating corruption anomalies with village fund absorption data? (RQ2) Which algorithm has the higher performance in discriminating anomaly, i.e., IF, LOF, and RDA? (RQ3) What are the algorithms’ anomalies according to the type of corruption?

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}


\subsection{Typologies for Corruption Classification}

Based on the typology derived from TASP model of Graycar \cite{ref19}, three empirical studies provide the basis for the present one: Siregar and Aminudin \cite{ref13} find five types in East Java; Kartadinata et al. \cite{ref14} classify prosecution cases of 200$+$ conducted by KPK into mark-up (\textit{penggelembungan}) and fictitious projects (\textit{proyek fiktif}); finally, Medan et al. \cite{ref20} confirm the same classification persists up until 2025 in East Nusa Tenggara.

\subsection{Fraud Triangle and Principal-Agent Theory Foundation}

Fraud Triangle proposed by Cressey \cite{ref21} and applied to the context of Dana Desa by Hidajat \cite{ref12} involves the identification of three components: Pressure (pressure to disburse and create negative motivation); Opportunity (absence of competitive bidding scheme and auditing); and Rationalization (financial manipulation is normalized). An overwhelming amount (98.8\%) of Swakelola procurement type confirms S{\o}reide's \cite{ref22} statement about absence of competitive bidding process.

\subsection{Public Financial Data-based Anomaly Detection}

In total, there exist three different paradigm-based methods which can be employed complementarily rather than substituted. First, the Isolation Forest (IF) method \cite{ref24,ref25} is based on global sparsity estimation with help of random partitioning path lengths. Second, Local Outlier Factor (LOF) method \cite{ref26} considers the difference between neighborhood densities of point and those of its $k$ nearest neighbours. It is capable of capturing within-category dynamics of prices which remain unseen by global methods. Lastly, Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA) \cite{ref27} incorporates sparse noise decomposition matrix for improvement of autoencoder training. According to Chandola et al. \cite{ref28}, methods using ensemble approaches which combine several paradigms have proved superior to their standalone counterparts. However, previous work on anomaly detection on Indonesian public financial data failed to apply all three paradigms to village funds activity records simultaneously \cite{ref29,ref30}.


\subsection{Theory Base for Information Systems}

The information systems success framework proposed by DeLone and McLean (2003)~\cite{ref31} forms the foundation for designing an anomaly detection pipeline to serve as an intervention in the IS realm. The outputs of the anomaly detection pipeline, such as scores, typologies, and village tiers, will serve as inputs into the Information Quality dimension that will in turn drive Individual Impact (auditor attention allocation) and Organizational Impact (anti-corruption efforts that address state losses). The work by Mutungi et al. ~\cite{ref11} indicates that digital anti-corruption interventions that do not consider corruption interaction points cannot affect corruption, thus lending support to the typology-based feature engineering utilized here.


\subsection{Operational Dimensions of KPK Prevention Programs}
\label{sec:kpk_prevention}

In the Trisula strategy ~\cite{ref4}, the KPK assigns the task of conducting prevention interventions after educating individuals and before enforcing policies. According to Stranas PK, in 2025-2026, the strategy of strengthening village governance represents one of the fifteen priorities, which will necessitate the compulsory introduction of the Siswaskeudes and inter-agency data interoperability between Kemendes, Kemendagri, Kemenkeu, and Bappenas ~\cite{ref7}.

\subsection{Dataset and Data Sources}

The current work employs expenditure logs of the degree of village funds utilization, obtained from jaga.id (\url{https://jaga.id}), the public monitoring website administered by the KPK, which enables public access to village fund expenditure absorption details on a provincial scale~\cite{ref9}. Data from two different sources have been merged by utilizing a composite key \texttt{Kode\_Desa} × \texttt{Tahun}. These are: (1) Penyerapan (expenditure absorption) data~\textit{Penyerapan}, indicating expenditures realized for each activity and at what stage of disbursement, and (2) Pagu (budget ceiling) data ~\textit{Pagu} , indicating budget ceilings approved for activities in villages. The longitudinal panel obtained this way contains \textbf{99,692 activity-level records} (33,140 for 2023; 36,151 for 2024; 30,401 for 2025), covering all kabupaten/kota in the Jambi Province. No existing fraudulent flags can be associated with any record.

The study employs expenditure logs showing the extent of village funds spending, available through the jaga.id website (\url{https://jaga.id}), which is a public monitoring portal run by the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK). It offers the public access to village fund absorption information at the province level [ref9]. Data from two different datasets have been combined by employing the combination of variables \texttt{Kode\_Desa} × \texttt{Tahun} as a composite key. These datasets are (i) Penyerapan (expenditure absorption) dataset, referring to the expenditures made per activity, along with the stage of disbursement, and (ii) Pagu (budget ceiling) dataset, referring to the budget ceiling per activity made in the villages. Thus, the final longitudinal panel consists of 99,692 activity-level observations (33,140 for 2023, 36,151 for 2024, and 30,401 for 2025), representing all districts/cities of the Jambi province.

\subsection{Detection Algorithms}

\textbf{Isolation Forest (IF)} partitions the data points with random splits along each feature axes; hence, shorter average path lengths result in anomalies ~\cite{ref24, ref25}.

The \textbf{Local Outlier Factor (LOF)} computes anomaly scores by estimating the local reachability density ratio between a data point and its k nearest neighbours; a LOF value greater than one implies that the outlier is locally sparse ~\cite{ref26}.

In \textbf{Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)}, the input X is decomposed into two components L and S, where L is a noise-free feature while S is noise (L1 penalty); hence, outliers cannot distort the learned normal manifold ~\cite{ref27}; reconstruction error defines the anomaly score. The complete set of parameters is provided in Figure ~\ref{fig:framework}.

\subsection{Consensus Framework and Typology Mapping}

If the record is marked by at least two ($\geq$\,2) out of three methods, then its consensus flag is set to \texttt{consensus\_flag = 1}. The records marked through consensus are later classified using a rule-based typology engine that associates feature sets to seven typology classes (T1-T7). The ontology includes three empirical datasets: the modus operandi typology from Siregar and Aminudin~\cite{ref13}; the systematic typology of over 200 KPK corruption cases developed by Kartadinata et al.~\cite{ref14}; and the anti-corruption mode from Hidajat. The rules for \textit{Unclassified} assigning typologies based on the features are described in Table 4 ~\ref{tab:typo_rules}.

\subsection{Classification of Priorities by Village Tier}

The village tier categorizes the flags from the activity tier into a persistency rating that covers three financial years. The first tier is designated High Priority if there are flags for two or more years. The second tier is Moderate if there is a flag for one year, and the third tier is Not Flagged if there are no flags in the three years covered. The persistence across multiple years embodies the Opportunity component of the Fraud Triangle \cite{ref21}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Detection Rates, Score Quality, and Inter-Method Agreement}

Table \ref{tab:rates} shows the numbers of flagged anomalies in each year for all three fiscal years. IQR's baseline threshold marks 18.5\% of the data, a rate which is inherently too high for focused auditing purposes. IF's high 2023 rate of 10.5\% (versus 6.5-7.1\% in later years) highlights the effect of post-pandemic budget growth, where higher spending budgets have encouraged exaggerated claims. LOF and RDA share a consistent total rate of 5.0\%. The multiple paradigm agreement (at least two out of three paradigms) flags 3,107 data points (3.1\%), significantly narrowing down the sample size compared to each individual paradigm.

Tables~\ref{tab:bc} and~\ref{tab:overlap} define score quality and inter-method agreement. The LOF is characterized by the most pronounced bimodal behavior (BC = 0.957), giving rise to clear boundaries separating the normal cluster from the outlier tail. Conversely, IF is unimodal (BC = 0.335), with the 95th percentile threshold lying within the highly dense part of the score distribution, reducing the reliability of each flag. The RDA falls into the middle ground (BC = 0.703). As for overlap, IF and RDA share 50.3\% of RDA flags, both detecting globally extreme outliers for multiple features (\texttt{cost\_per\_unit}  102.83$\sigma$; \texttt{cost\_deviation\_by\_category} 42.42$\sigma$). On the other hand, LOF shows the lowest degree of overlap with both IF (6.4\%) and RDA (12.0\%) since it detects outliers structurally different from those found using the other two methods (in terms of local density deviation).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Algorithm Selection: LOF's Superiority Is Theoretically Predicted}

The bimodality coefficient value of 0.957 in LOF can be predicted based on the structural nature of corruption in the Siskeudes dataset. Road building, PAUD operations (Religion-based Kindergarten), and BLT (\textit{Bantuan Langsung Tunai}) cash distributions form natural clusters within the same \texttt{Kode\_Output} code because these activities have similar cost ranges, procurement forms, and stages. In case when one of the fraudulent records falls into such clusters 42.42 standard deviations above the mean cost of its own cluster, LOF successfully detects the outlier, despite the fact that IF considers it a globally not unusual record using path-partitioning~\cite{ref26}. The unimodal distribution in IF (BC = 0.335) is caused by the structural nature of the data—the corruption occurs in clusters per different activities and not globally randomly scattered in the score space to enable a bimodal distribution. The heterogeneity issue is solved in the proposed framework by considering the IF $\cap$ RDA intersection (50.3\%, n = 2,506), which finds the globally most anomalous records supported by two independent global approaches, LOF-specific local deviations from each category, and 156 triple consensus records—the most reliable indicators found across all algorithms.

\subsection{Fraud Triangle in the Data}

All three conditions can be seen quantitatively from the results. \textit{Pressure} exist in the form of a high anomaly rate for IF in 2023 (10.5\%) as compared to following years, i.e., 6.5–7.1\%, which is due to the post-COVID expansion of budget resources for village disbursement targets, which resulted in incentivized exaggerated claim making. \textit{Opportunity} has been supported by the prevalence of Swakelola procurement methods at 98.8\% rate, which, according to the structural condition defined by Søreide~\cite{ref22}, is the main enabling factor, as such activities lack any competitive tendering process, price checking process, or external audit before disbursement. The existence of 642 T1 villages (47.1\%) shows the persistence of opportunity in contrast to the incidental occurrence of anomalies, as the same villages exhibit irregularity in multiple years of the financial year. \textit{Rationalisation} is indicated from the distribution pattern of the typologies, whereby the T1 (n = 1,571) and T7 (n = 1,568) schemes are more prevalent compared to the complex operational schemes, i.e., T3 = 38 and T5 = 26~\cite{ref21}.

\subsection{Principal-Agent Asymmetry, Subthreshold Masking, and Audit Intelligence}

The prevalent feature in terms of RDA error, \texttt{avg\_completion}, captures the exact location where the influence of information asymmetry is strongest ~\cite{ref23}. Reports show that village leaders claim complete realization of T1, with a decrease in the subsequent T2 and T3 tranches, trending towards zero, which causes an error with the maximum magnitude (mean squared error $\approx$ 0.00145) as there cannot be any reasonable absorption behavior that results in such a pattern. Such vulnerability exists due to the fact that the APIP inspection cycle typically ends once the disbursement windows for T3 are closed. Thus, the proposed RDA decomposition advises that the process of verification should start from checking the completion percentage reports against physical output on the ground, before examining procurement documentation.

There are 708 unclassified consensus records (22.8\% of the dataset) that require special consideration, as opposed to classification residuals. These observations fall into a multi-paradigm category—marked as abnormal by two or more($\geq$\,2) different approaches—but do not satisfy individual thresholds for all rules of typology. The most likely explanation lies in combined fraud: those transactions that involve moderate mark-ups and partial completion manipulation such that no one feature crosses the relevant threshold on its own but their combination is still anomalous. As for the APIP project, these observations should be carefully analyzed in conjunction across multiple dimensions, making an extensive desk review the necessary precursor to field visits.

\subsection{DeLone and McLean Evaluation and Institutional Positioning}

The framework developed by DeLone and McLean, focuses on the evaluation of three dimensions \cite{ref31}. Information Quality: validation of the consensus scoring, typology labeling, and tiered priorities based on multi-paradigm convergence, alignment with known behavior of the judicial system \cite{ref13,ref14}, and empirical evidence from the Jambi prosecution case studies that have confirmed targeted financial anomalies in verified cases of fraud \cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. Individual Impact: by reducing APIP’s search space with the ranking to 93.5\% \cite{ref25}, the framework addresses the constraint posed by insufficient staff members as described by Srirejeki and Faturokhman \cite{ref29}. Organisational Impact: achieving this impact entails the integration of pipeline output into APIP’s inspectorate processes as pre-inspection intelligence—an organisational design problem requiring process change management and inspector training.

It is particularly pertinent to consider the institution of the pipeline in light of the detection gap found in the KPK diagnostic: the two to five-year delay in the Jambi prosecution case study indicates that the prevention channel called Trisula \cite{ref4} is not performing activity-level automated screening during the expenditure realisation year. In the cases where Siswaskeudes has been adopted~\cite{ref6}, typology labelled outputs serve as pre-prioritisation filters; otherwise, the outputs themselves provide automated activity screening TABK (\textit{Teknik Audit Berbantuan Komputer}) and make up the Siswaskeudes adoption business case. MCP’s Tata Kelola Dana Desa governance scoring system considers the input and process indicators, whereas the pipeline considers output indicators, implying that the kabupaten’s MCP score and proportion of Tier-1 villages reveal the divergence between procedural compliance and outcome, providing diagnostic information in its own right. For \textit{Desa Antikorupsi} initiative \cite{ref5}, the 263 Tier-3 villages (19.3\%) with three successive anomaly-free fiscal years provide a pool of empirical candidates for the recognition program, which is scaled to 33 villages nationally due to coverage constraints, into a scaled accountability recognition system.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, a comparison unsupervised machine learning pipeline is developed and analyzed based on 99,692 village fund expenditure observations at activity level from the province of Jambi (Fiscal Year 2023-2025). The research is motivated by three empirical questions.

\textbf{RQ1}: Seven modus-operandi-grounded features~\cite{ref13,ref14} discriminate anomalies without prior fraud labels. \texttt{avg\_completion} dominates RDA reconstruction error (MSE $\approx$ 0.00145); extreme skew in \texttt{cost\_per\_unit} ($102.83\sigma$) confirms price inflation is directly traceable in Siskeudes absorption data.

\textbf{RQ2}: LOF achieves the highest BC (0.957) through within-category local density comparison; global methods IF (BC\,=\,0.335) and RDA (BC\,=\,0.703) miss cluster-internal outliers. The multi-paradigm consensus yields 3,107 records that no single method identifies alone.

\textbf{RQ3}: Consensus anomalies concentrate in T1 Mark-up (50.6\%) and T7 Cross-Category Dump (50.5\%), consistent with Jambi judicial records~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18} and prior typology studies~\cite{ref13,ref14}. The 708 unclassified records (22.8\%) signal compound fraud undetectable by single-threshold rules.

Pipeline operationalises the DeLone and McLean Information Quality-to-Organisational Impact model~\cite{ref31} by compressing the search space in APIP’s absence of pre-labelled cases down from 99,692 observations to 642 priority villages, which equates to a 93.5\% compression ratio. Labeled outputs in terms of typology—mark-ups, budget migration across categories, and inconsistency at each stage—may be used for preliminary inquiry targeting of KPK and Kejaksaan, which may allow for detection of the two to five years observed lag in Jambi criminal prosecutions ~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. Pipeline fulfils the automated activity-level screening role lacking from current prevention infrastructure~\cite{ref7}; its Tier-1 output guides prioritisation of Siswaskeudes TABK~\cite{ref6}; its Tier-3 output provides ground truth for empirical selection of \textit{Desa Antikorupsi} ~\cite{ref5}; and its typology outputs allow for preliminary inquiry within the fiscal year when expenditure realisation takes place, which improves Trisula prevention capability~\cite{ref4} at granularities where current tools are absent.

\textbf{Limitations}: (1) Limitation to Jambi province makes generalisation only possible for provinces with similar disbursement characteristics. (2) jaga.id data have been extracted from the entries made by villages in Siskeudes, thus systematic misreporting at the entry phase represents an undetectable quality risk. (3) Activity-level ground truth labels not available means inability to conduct any precision-recall performance evaluation of results. (4) IF contamination rate of 5\% is a hypothetical prior; incorrect calibration may move decision boundary into denser areas of distribution. (5) Single-threshold typology rules are unable to handle compound fraud at moderate joint anomalies. (6) LOF $O(n^2)$ time complexity restricts scalability to approximately 100,000 records without approximate search optimization.

\textbf{Future directions}: (1) Link pipeline-detected behavior with KPK indictments to create an annotated dataset for testing the classification accuracy of the typology. (2) Establish contamination percentages through expert annotation of APIP samples. (3) Expand the analysis to cover the years 2018-2025 or another set of provinces, with a natural language method applied to enhance persistence detection abilities and study corruption development throughout the maturation phases of Dana Desa.
