\begin{abstract}
The Indonesian Dana Desa scheme distributes around Rp~71 trillion each year to 75,259 villages, while the recorded fraudulent activities, comprising 640 cases amounting to Rp 598.13 billion in confirmed state loss, reveal the existence of a persistent detection problem. While detailed activity-based expenditure data are kept in Siskeudes, there is no pre-inspection automatic screening system currently functioning at the provincial level. In this research, an unsupervised machine learning pipeline composed of Isolation Forest (IF), Local Outlier Factor (LOF), and Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA) was used to examine 99,692 activity-level expenditure data from Jambi Province between fiscal years 2023-2025 from jaga.id. The seven-typology features that represent the documented modus operandi are used for the analysis. By implementing a multi-paradigm consensus strategy, which requires agreement from at least two of three methods, 3,107 anomalous cases (3.1\%) were identified. The LOF method produced the best bimodality coefficient (BC = 0.957). The marked anomalies fall into two typologies, namely Mark-up (T1: 50.6\%) and Cross-Category Dump (T7: 50.5\%), which corresponds to the court data. After examining 10 villages, six of them were classified as high-priority (47.1\%). The machine learning pipeline shrinks the APIP inspection workload of 99,692 cases down to 642 cases, resulting in a 93.5\% search space reduction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Indonesia's Dana Desa is one of the world's largest intergovernmental fiscal transfers to sub-national governments, transferring around Rp 71 trillion per year into 75,259 villages according to Law No. 6 of 2014 on Villages~\cite{ref1}. ICW identified a total of 591 corruption court judgments on village fund misallocation until 2024, involving 640 perpetrators and resulting in verified state losses of Rp 598.13 billion~\cite{ref2}. Meanwhile, KPK has detected 851 cases of village funds misappropriations since 2015, with village heads responsible for over 60\% of all perpetrators~\cite{ref3}. It is clear that the dominant approach towards village funds corruption has been largely a post-hoc prosecution rather than pro-active and data-driven approaches.

The Sistem Keuangan Desa (Siskeudes), hosted by BPKP, documents village fund expenditure in terms of specific activities through all stages of disbursements: planning activity type, budget allocation, realization per tranche (T1, T2, T3), and procurement method. This expenditure information forms an extensive administrative signals database, where certain patterns of expenditures are encoded implicitly to distinguish between corruption-free expenditure activities and suspicious ones. Yet, there are no systematic automated screening systems that query this database for any anomalies prior to completing the cycle of inspection and closure of the disbursement window.

This detection gap persists despite KPK's Trisula prevention strategy~\cite{ref4} and four village-focused instruments (\textit{Desa Antikorupsi}, Siswaskeudes, MCP, jaga.id~\cite{ref5,ref6,ref7,ref8,ref9}); as Section~\ref{sec:kpk_prevention} details, none provide automated activity-level screening within the disbursement year.

The existing literature mostly focuses on retrospective analysis ~\cite{ref10}, governance accountability frameworks ~\cite{ref11}, and triangle fraud diagnostic approaches~\cite{ref12}. Machine learning applications, which can be found, mostly apply to supervised classification approaches, which cannot be used due to absence of labels in the monitoring context~\cite{ref13,ref14}. To overcome this limitation, an unsupervised pipeline will be constructed to detect anomalies in the expenditure records of village funds in Jambi Province during fiscal years 2023-2025.

The Jambi Province serves as an empirically important case for structural analysis. Between 2024 and 2026, jaga.id reported only eleven complaints concerning Jambi (1.4\% out of 761 complaints nationwide), in comparison with similar provinces like Sumatera Utara, where there were eighty-one complaints~\cite{ref9}. The underrepresentation of voluntary complaints suggests that there is no adequate monitoring environment but not necessarily an unexposed corruption problem: four parallel court cases in Jambi province confirm documented loss amounts of Rp 2.301 billion occurring in the districts of Kerinci, Muaro Jambi, and Tanjung Jabung Timur with detection delays of two to five years~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. This research formulates three research questions: (RQ1) What is the feature construct that can best discriminate corruption anomalies based on village fund absorption data? (RQ2) What is the better-performing algorithm in terms of discriminating anomaly, namely IF, LOF, or RDA? (RQ3) What are the algorithmic anomalies in accordance with corruption types?

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Typologies for Corruption Classification}

Grounded in Graycar's TASP model~\cite{ref19}, three empirical studies anchor the typology used here: Siregar and Aminudin~\cite{ref13} identify five schemes in East Java; Kartadinata et al.~\cite{ref14} classify 200$+$ KPK prosecution cases into mark-up (\textit{penggelembungan}) and fictitious projects (\textit{proyek fiktif}); Medan et al.~\cite{ref20} confirm the same patterns persist through 2025 in East Nusa Tenggara.

\subsection{Fraud Triangle and Principal-Agent Theory Foundation}

The Fraud Triangle, proposed by Cressey~\cite{ref21}, and used in the context of Dana Desa by Hidajat~\cite{ref12}, relies on identifying three elements: Pressure (disbursement pressure leading to adverse motives), Opportunity (absence of competitive bidding structure and auditing), and Rationalization (normalizing financial manipulation). The overwhelming proportion of Swakelola procurement type (98.8\%) confirms the claim of S{\o}reide~\cite{ref22} that the lack of competitive bidding process.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection in Public Financial Data}

There are three distinct methodological paradigms which operate complementarily, as opposed to substitutively. First, the Isolation Forest (IF)~\cite{ref24,ref25} is based on a global sparsity measurement using random partitioning path length. The second is the Local Outlier Factor (LOF)~\cite{ref26}, which recognizes anomalies based on neighbourhood density dissimilarity compared to that of the $k$-nearest neighbors. It captures the within-category price inflation dynamics which remain invisible to the global approaches. Thirdly, the Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)~\cite{ref27} improves the autoencoder training with the sparse noise decomposition matrix. According to Chandola et al.~\cite{ref28}, ensemble methods which combine several paradigms have shown better results compared to singular methods. Previous research related to the application of anomaly detection on Indonesian public financial data has not utilized the combination of all three paradigms on village funds activity level records~\cite{ref29,ref30}.

\subsection{Information Systems Grounding}

The information system success model proposed by DeLone and McLean~\cite{ref31} in 2003 underpins the motivation for creating an anomaly detection pipeline as an intervention at the IS level. Pipeline results (scores, typologies, and village tiers) form the Information Quality dimension, which influences both Individual Impact (auditor attention allocation) and Organizational Impact (anti-corruption measures preventing state losses). Mutungi et al.~\cite{ref11} prove that digital interventions which do not address corruption interaction points have no effect on corruption, thus proving the soundness of typology-driven feature engineering methodology applied herein.

\subsection{Operational dimensions of related KPK Anti Corruption Programs}
\label{sec:kpk_prevention}

Within the Trisula strategy~\cite{ref4}, KPK positions the prevention phase between education and enforcement efforts. For the 2025-2026 period, Stranas PK lists "strengthening village governance" as one of fifteen priority actions, recommending the mandatory implementation of Siswaskeudes and cross-governmental collaboration for data integration within Kemendes, Kemendagri, Kemenkeu, and Bappenas ~\cite{ref7}.

\subsection{Dataset and Data Sources}

The current work employs expenditure logs of the degree of village funds utilization, obtained from jaga.id (\url{https://jaga.id}), the public monitoring website administered by the KPK, which enables public access to village fund expenditure absorption details on a provincial scale~\cite{ref9}. Data from two different sources have been merged by utilizing a composite key \texttt{Kode\_Desa} × \texttt{Tahun}. These are: (1) Penyerapan (expenditure absorption) data~\textit{Penyerapan}, indicating expenditures realized for each activity and at what stage of disbursement, and (2) Pagu (budget ceiling) data ~\textit{Pagu} , indicating budget ceilings approved for activities in villages. The longitudinal panel obtained this way contains \textbf{99,692 activity-level records} (33,140 for 2023; 36,151 for 2024; 30,401 for 2025), covering all kabupaten/kota in the Jambi Province. No existing fraudulent flags can be associated with any record.

\subsection{Detection Algorithms}

\textbf{Isolation Forest (IF)} isolates records via random axis-aligned splits; shorter average path length indicates higher anomaly likelihood~\cite{ref24,ref25}.

\textbf{Local Outlier Factor (LOF)} scores records by the ratio of local reachability density to that of their $k$-nearest neighbours; LOF $> 1$ signals a locally sparse outlier~\cite{ref26}.

\textbf{Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)} decomposes input $X$ into a clean representation $L$ and a sparse noise matrix $S$ (L1 penalty), preventing outliers from corrupting the learned normal manifold~\cite{ref27}; anomaly score is reconstruction MSE. Full hyperparameter settings are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:framework}.

\subsection{Consensus Framework and Typology Mapping}

A record receives \texttt{consensus\_flag = 1} if flagged by $\geq$\,2 of 3 methods. Consensus-flagged records are post-processed through a rule-based typology module mapping feature profiles to seven typology labels (T1--T7). The schema synthesises three empirical sources: Siregar and Aminudin's modus operandi taxonomy~\cite{ref13}, Kartadinata et al.'s systematic classification of 200$+$ KPK prosecution cases~\cite{ref14}, and Hidajat's anti-corruption mode analysis~\cite{ref12}. Table~\ref{tab:typo_rules} presents the full feature-to-typology assignment rules. Records satisfying no individual rule threshold receive an \textit{Unclassified} assignment.

\subsection{Classification of Priorities by Village Tier}

The village tier categorizes the flags from the activity tier into a persistency rating that covers three financial years. The first tier is designated High Priority if there are flags for two or more years. The second tier is Moderate if there is a flag for one year, and the third tier is Not Flagged if there are no flags in the three years covered. The persistence across multiple years embodies the Opportunity component of the Fraud Triangle \cite{ref21}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Detection Rates, Score Quality, and Inter-Method Agreement}

Table~\ref{tab:rates} reports anomaly flag counts across all three fiscal years. The IQR baseline flags 18.5\% of records---structurally too broad for targeted inspection use. IF's elevated 2023 rate (10.5\% versus 6.5--7.1\% in subsequent years) reflects post-COVID budget expansion, when enlarged allocations incentivised inflated claims. LOF and RDA converge on a stable 5.0\% overall rate. The multi-paradigm consensus ($\geq$\,2 of 3 methods) yields 3,107 records (3.1\%)---a materially tighter target set than any single method alone.

Tables~\ref{tab:bc} and~\ref{tab:overlap} characterise score quality and inter-method convergence. LOF achieves the strongest bimodal separation (BC\,=\,0.957), producing a sharp boundary between the normal cluster and the anomaly tail. IF's unimodal distribution (BC\,=\,0.335) means its 95th-percentile threshold intersects a high-density score region, reducing individual flag confidence. RDA occupies an intermediate position (BC\,=\,0.703). On pairwise overlap, IF and RDA share 50.3\% of RDA's flags---both respond to globally extreme multi-feature deviations (\texttt{cost\_per\_unit} at 102.83$\sigma$; \texttt{cost\_deviation\_by\_category} at 42.42$\sigma$). LOF's low overlap with IF (6.4\%) and RDA (12.0\%) confirms it captures a structurally distinct subset through local density comparison. The 156 triple-consensus records constitute the highest-confidence fraud signals across all modalities.

\subsection{Corruption Typology and Village Priority Distribution}

Using multi-label assignment (a record may satisfy multiple typology rules simultaneously), Table~\ref{tab:typology} shows that T1 (Mark-up, 50.6\%) and T7 (Cross-Category Dump, 50.5\%) co-dominate the 3,107 consensus-flagged records, consistent with Jambi judicial records~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18} and prior typology studies~\cite{ref13,ref14}. T2 (Ghost Activity, 24.9\%) ranks third; operationally complex schemes (T3, T5, T6) remain rare. T4 (Stage Lock) registers zero detections---fully undisbursed budgets fall below the consensus threshold, a subthreshold masking pattern discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. The 708 unclassified records (22.8\%) pass the multi-paradigm threshold yet satisfy no single typology rule, pointing to compound fraud where no individual feature exceeds its standalone threshold. At the village level (Table~\ref{tab:village_tiers}), 642 villages (47.1\%) qualify as Tier~1 with flags in $\geq$\,2 fiscal years---a prevalence inconsistent with incidental error and indicative of entrenched structural irregularity.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Algorithm Selection: LOF's Superiority Is Theoretically Predicted}

The bimodality coefficient value of 0.957 in LOF can be predicted based on the structural nature of corruption in the Siskeudes dataset. Road building, PAUD operations (Religion-based Kindergarten), and BLT (\textit{Bantuan Langsung Tunai}) cash distributions form natural clusters within the same \texttt{Kode\_Output} code because these activities have similar cost ranges, procurement forms, and stages. In case when one of the fraudulent records falls into such clusters 42.42 standard deviations above the mean cost of its own cluster, LOF successfully detects the outlier, despite the fact that IF considers it a globally not unusual record using path-partitioning~\cite{ref26}. The unimodal distribution in IF (BC = 0.335) is caused by the structural nature of the data—the corruption occurs in clusters per different activities and not globally randomly scattered in the score space to enable a bimodal distribution. The heterogeneity issue is solved in the proposed framework by considering the IF $\cap$ RDA intersection (50.3\%, n = 2,506), which finds the globally most anomalous records supported by two independent global approaches, LOF-specific local deviations from each category, and 156 triple consensus records—the most reliable indicators found across all algorithms.

\subsection{Fraud Triangle in the Data}

All three conditions can be seen quantitatively from the results. \textit{Pressure} exist in the form of a high anomaly rate for IF in 2023 (10.5\%) as compared to following years, i.e., 6.5–7.1\%, which is due to the post-COVID expansion of budget resources for village disbursement targets, which resulted in incentivized exaggerated claim making. \textit{Opportunity} has been supported by the prevalence of Swakelola procurement methods at 98.8\% rate, which, according to the structural condition defined by Søreide~\cite{ref22}, is the main enabling factor, as such activities lack any competitive tendering process, price checking process, or external audit before disbursement. The existence of 642 T1 villages (47.1\%) shows the persistence of opportunity in contrast to the incidental occurrence of anomalies, as the same villages exhibit irregularity in multiple years of the financial year. \textit{Rationalisation} is indicated from the distribution pattern of the typologies, whereby the T1 (n = 1,571) and T7 (n = 1,568) schemes are more prevalent compared to the complex operational schemes, i.e., T3 = 38 and T5 = 26~\cite{ref21}.

\subsection{Principal-Agent Asymmetry, Subthreshold Masking, and Audit Intelligence}

The prevalent feature in terms of RDA error, \texttt{avg\_completion}, captures the exact location where the influence of information asymmetry is strongest ~\cite{ref23}. Reports show that village leaders claim complete realization of T1, with a decrease in the subsequent T2 and T3 tranches, trending towards zero, which causes an error with the maximum magnitude (mean squared error $\approx$ 0.00145) as there cannot be any reasonable absorption behavior that results in such a pattern. Such vulnerability exists due to the fact that the APIP inspection cycle typically ends once the disbursement windows for T3 are closed. Thus, the proposed RDA decomposition advises that the process of verification should start from checking the completion percentage reports against physical output on the ground, before examining procurement documentation.

There are 708 unclassified consensus records (22.8\% of the dataset) that require special consideration, as opposed to classification residuals. These observations fall into a multi-paradigm category—marked as abnormal by two or more($\geq$\,2) different approaches—but do not satisfy individual thresholds for all rules of typology. The most likely explanation lies in combined fraud: those transactions that involve moderate mark-ups and partial completion manipulation such that no one feature crosses the relevant threshold on its own but their combination is still anomalous. As for the APIP project, these observations should be carefully analyzed in conjunction across multiple dimensions, making an extensive desk review the necessary precursor to field visits.

\subsection{DeLone and McLean Evaluation and Institutional Positioning}

The framework developed by DeLone and McLean, focuses on the evaluation of three dimensions \cite{ref31}. Information Quality: validation of the consensus scoring, typology labeling, and tiered priorities based on multi-paradigm convergence, alignment with known behavior of the judicial system \cite{ref13,ref14}, and empirical evidence from the Jambi prosecution case studies that have confirmed targeted financial anomalies in verified cases of fraud \cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. Individual Impact: by reducing APIP’s search space with the ranking to 93.5\% \cite{ref25}, the framework addresses the constraint posed by insufficient staff members as described by Srirejeki and Faturokhman \cite{ref29}. Organisational Impact: achieving this impact entails the integration of pipeline output into APIP’s inspectorate processes as pre-inspection intelligence—an organisational design problem requiring process change management and inspector training.

It is particularly pertinent to consider the institution of the pipeline in light of the detection gap found in the KPK diagnostic: the two to five-year delay in the Jambi prosecution case study indicates that the prevention channel called Trisula \cite{ref4} is not performing activity-level automated screening during the expenditure realisation year. In the cases where Siswaskeudes has been adopted~\cite{ref6}, typology labelled outputs serve as pre-prioritisation filters; otherwise, the outputs themselves provide automated activity screening TABK (\textit{Teknik Audit Berbantuan Komputer}) and make up the Siswaskeudes adoption business case. MCP’s Tata Kelola Dana Desa governance scoring system considers the input and process indicators, whereas the pipeline considers output indicators, implying that the kabupaten’s MCP score and proportion of Tier-1 villages reveal the divergence between procedural compliance and outcome, providing diagnostic information in its own right. For \textit{Desa Antikorupsi} initiative \cite{ref5}, the 263 Tier-3 villages (19.3\%) with three successive anomaly-free fiscal years provide a pool of empirical candidates for the recognition program, which is scaled to 33 villages nationally due to coverage constraints, into a scaled accountability recognition system.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this work, a comparison unsupervised machine learning pipeline is developed and analyzed based on 99,692 village fund expenditure observations at activity level from the province of Jambi (Fiscal Year 2023-2025). The research is motivated by three empirical questions.

\textbf{RQ1}: Seven modus-operandi-grounded features~\cite{ref13,ref14} discriminate anomalies without prior fraud labels. \texttt{avg\_completion} dominates RDA reconstruction error (MSE $\approx$ 0.00145); extreme skew in \texttt{cost\_per\_unit} ($102.83\sigma$) confirms price inflation is directly traceable in Siskeudes absorption data.

\textbf{RQ2}: LOF achieves the highest BC (0.957) through within-category local density comparison; global methods IF (BC\,=\,0.335) and RDA (BC\,=\,0.703) miss cluster-internal outliers. The multi-paradigm consensus yields 3,107 records that no single method identifies alone.

\textbf{RQ3}: Consensus anomalies concentrate in T1 Mark-up (50.6\%) and T7 Cross-Category Dump (50.5\%), consistent with Jambi judicial records~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18} and prior typology studies~\cite{ref13,ref14}. The 708 unclassified records (22.8\%) signal compound fraud undetectable by single-threshold rules.

Pipeline operationalises the DeLone and McLean Information Quality-to-Organisational Impact model~\cite{ref31} by compressing the search space in APIP’s absence of pre-labelled cases down from 99,692 observations to 642 priority villages, which equates to a 93.5\% compression ratio. Labeled outputs in terms of typology—mark-ups, budget migration across categories, and inconsistency at each stage—may be used for preliminary inquiry targeting of KPK and Kejaksaan, which may allow for detection of the two to five years observed lag in Jambi criminal prosecutions ~\cite{ref15,ref16,ref17,ref18}. Pipeline fulfils the automated activity-level screening role lacking from current prevention infrastructure~\cite{ref7}; its Tier-1 output guides prioritisation of Siswaskeudes TABK~\cite{ref6}; its Tier-3 output provides ground truth for empirical selection of \textit{Desa Antikorupsi} ~\cite{ref5}; and its typology outputs allow for preliminary inquiry within the fiscal year when expenditure realisation takes place, which improves Trisula prevention capability~\cite{ref4} at granularities where current tools are absent.

\textbf{Limitations}: (1) Limitation to Jambi province makes generalisation only possible for provinces with similar disbursement characteristics. (2) jaga.id data have been extracted from the entries made by villages in Siskeudes, thus systematic misreporting at the entry phase represents an undetectable quality risk. (3) Activity-level ground truth labels not available means inability to conduct any precision-recall performance evaluation of results. (4) IF contamination rate of 5\% is a hypothetical prior; incorrect calibration may move decision boundary into denser areas of distribution. (5) Single-threshold typology rules are unable to handle compound fraud at moderate joint anomalies. (6) LOF $O(n^2)$ time complexity restricts scalability to approximately 100,000 records without approximate search optimization.

\textbf{Future directions}: (1) Match pipeline flagged activities with KPK indictments to generate annotated training data for empirical assessment of typology classification. (2) Adjust contamination rates based on expert annotation of APIP inspected sample. (3) Expand the analysis to the Fiscal Year 2018–2025 or other province dataset with natural language approach to enhance persistence-detection capabilities and examine corruption evolution across Dana Desa maturation process stages.
