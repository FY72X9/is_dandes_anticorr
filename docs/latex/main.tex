\begin{abstract}
Indonesia's Dana Desa programme disburses approximately Rp~71 trillion annually to 75,259 villages, yet documented fraud spanning 640 defendants and Rp~598.13 billion in verified state losses reveals a persistent detection gap. Despite granular activity-level expenditure records maintained in Siskeudes, no automated pre-inspection screening mechanism operates at the provincial level. This study develops a three-method unsupervised machine learning pipeline---Isolation Forest (IF), Local Outlier Factor (LOF), and Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)---applied to 99,692 activity-level village fund expenditure records from Jambi Province across fiscal years 2023--2025, sourced via jaga.id. Seven typology-grounded features operationalise documented corruption modus operandi. A multi-paradigm consensus framework (agreement from $\geq$\,2 of 3 methods) identifies 3,107 high-confidence anomalous records (3.1\%). LOF achieves the highest bimodality coefficient (BC\,=\,0.957), demonstrating superior within-category anomaly discrimination. Consensus anomalies map predominantly to Mark-up (T1: 50.6\%) and Cross-Category Dump (T7: 50.5\%) typologies, consistent with judicial prosecution records. Village-level persistence analysis classifies 642 villages (47.1\%) as high-priority. The pipeline reduces the APIP inspection search space from 99,692 records to 642 priority villages---a 93.5\% reduction---without ground-truth labels, operationalising the DeLone and McLean IS Success Model for proactive anti-corruption triage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Indonesia's Dana Desa (Village Fund) programme represents one of the largest intergovernmental fiscal transfers in the developing world, channelling approximately Rp~71 trillion annually to 75,259 villages since the enactment of Law No.~6 of 2014 on Villages~\cite{ref1}. By 2024, Indonesian Corruption Watch (ICW) catalogued 591 court verdicts involving village fund misappropriation, implicating 640 defendants and documenting Rp~598.13 billion in verified state losses~\cite{ref2}. The Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK) identified 851 village fund corruption cases from 2015 onward, in which village heads accounted for more than 60\% of perpetrators~\cite{ref3}. These figures converge on a single diagnostic conclusion: the dominant response to village fund fraud remains reactive---prosecution occurring years after diversion---rather than proactive and data-driven.

The Sistem Keuangan Desa (Siskeudes), maintained by BPKP, records village fund expenditure at the activity level across each disbursement stage: planned activity type, budget allocation, realisation per tranche (T1, T2, T3), and procurement mechanism. This granular absorption data constitutes an administrative signal archive that encodes, in latent form, financial patterns distinguishing legitimate from corrupt expenditure. Yet no systematic automated screening mechanism currently operates at the district or provincial level to interrogate this archive before inspection cycles conclude and disbursement windows close.

Existing research concentrates on retrospective forensic analysis~\cite{ref4}, governance accountability frameworks~\cite{ref5}, and fraud triangle diagnostics~\cite{ref6}. Where machine learning enters the literature, it applies predominantly supervised classification---a paradigm requiring labelled ground truth unavailable in real-time monitoring contexts~\cite{ref13,ref14}. This study addresses this gap: it develops an unsupervised learning pipeline that detects expenditure anomalies in Jambi Province's village fund records across FY~2023--2025 without pre-existing fraud labels.

Jambi Province provides a structurally significant research site. jaga.id---the KPK-operated public transparency portal for village fund accountability---recorded only 11 complaint submissions attributed to Jambi Province (1.4\% of 761 national reports), while provinces of comparable scale, such as Sumatera Utara, registered 81~\cite{ref27}. Low voluntary reporting does not signal low fraud exposure; it signals a low-monitoring environment. Four contemporaneous Jambi prosecution cases document Rp~2.301 billion in verified losses across Kerinci, Muaro Jambi, and Tanjung Jabung Timur kabupaten~\cite{ref30,ref31,ref32,ref33}, with detection lags of two to five years. This study poses three research questions: (RQ1)~Which feature constructs derived from village fund expenditure absorption data discriminate corruption anomalies most effectively? (RQ2)~Which algorithm---IF, LOF, or RDA---demonstrates superior anomaly discrimination? (RQ3)~How do algorithmically identified anomalies map to established corruption typologies?

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Corruption Typology Frameworks}

Graycar's TASP framework demands that corruption analysis specify form, activity, sector, and context---making typology placement a precondition for targeted controls~\cite{ref7}. For the Dana Desa context, Siregar and Aminudin~\cite{ref13} provide the most relevant empirical classification: a multi-case analysis identifying five modus operandi---mark-up, fictitious budget items, double budgeting, procurement manipulation, and misuse of personnel funds. Kartadinata et al.~\cite{ref14} extend this taxonomy across 200+ KPK prosecution cases (2015--2020), confirming mark-up (\textit{penggelembungan}) and fictitious projects (\textit{proyek fiktif}) as dominant patterns. Medan et al.~\cite{ref15} confirm these modus operandi persist into 2025 in East Nusa Tenggara, validating the continuing relevance of typology-grounded detection.

\subsection{Fraud Triangle and Principal-Agent Foundations}

Cressey's Fraud Triangle~\cite{ref17}, operationalised for the Dana Desa context by Hidajat~\cite{ref6}, provides a three-condition model: Pressure (disbursement targets creating perverse incentives), Opportunity (structural absence of competitive procurement and limited auditor reach), and Rationalisation (normalisation of fund manipulation as low-risk). The 98.8\% Swakelola procurement dominance documented in the present dataset confirms S{\o}reide's~\cite{ref9} argument that the absence of competitive bidding is the primary structural enabler of procurement-stage corruption. Principal-agent theory~\cite{ref26} frames the village head as an agent exploiting information asymmetry relative to the principal chain: unexplained deviations from expected spending patterns constitute proximate evidence of this asymmetry exploitation---precisely the signal unsupervised anomaly detection targets.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection in Public Financial Data}

Three methodologically distinct paradigms have converged as complementary rather than substitutable in the anomaly detection literature. Isolation Forest (IF)~\cite{ref19,ref20} applies random partitioning path length as a global sparsity measure. Local Outlier Factor (LOF)~\cite{ref25} operates on local density ratios, identifying records whose neighbourhood density differs from their $k$-nearest peers---an architecture that captures within-category price inflation patterns that global methods structurally miss. The Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)~\cite{ref34} augments standard autoencoder training with a sparse noise decomposition matrix, preventing anomalous records from corrupting the learned normal representation. Chandola et al.~\cite{ref24} establish that multi-paradigm approaches consistently outperform any single method. Prior work on Indonesian public financial data has not applied this three-paradigm ensemble to village fund activity-level records~\cite{ref12,ref16}.

\subsection{Information Systems Grounding}

The DeLone and McLean IS Success Model~\cite{ref10}---in its 2003 formulation---provides the IS-level justification for deploying an anomaly detection pipeline as an institutional intervention. The pipeline's outputs (anomaly scores, typology labels, village priority tiers) constitute the Information Quality dimension, driving Individual Impact (auditor attention allocation) and Organisational Impact (corruption deterrence and state loss prevention). Mutungi et al.~\cite{ref5} demonstrate that digital anti-corruption tools fail when their design does not map to specific corruption interaction points---validating the typology-grounded feature engineering approach adopted in this study.

\subsection{Dataset and Data Sources}

The study uses activity-level village fund expenditure absorption records collected via jaga.id (\url{https://jaga.id})---the KPK-operated public monitoring portal for village fund transparency, providing open access to village fund expenditure realization records at the provincial level~\cite{ref27}. Two complementary sources were merged via composite key \texttt{Kode\_Desa} $\times$ \texttt{Tahun}: (1)~\textit{Penyerapan} (expenditure absorption) records documenting realised spending per activity per disbursement stage, and (2)~\textit{Pagu} (budget ceiling) records documenting approved allocations. The final longitudinal panel contains \textbf{99,692 activity-level records} (33,140 in 2023; 36,151 in 2024; 30,401 in 2025), covering all kabupaten/kota in Jambi Province. No pre-existing fraud labels attach to any record; the detection problem is entirely unsupervised.

\subsection{Detection Algorithms}

\textbf{Isolation Forest (IF)} partitions the feature space via random axis-aligned splits~\cite{ref19,ref20}. Records requiring fewer partitions to isolate receive lower anomaly scores. Contamination = 5\%; $n_{\text{estimators}} = 200$; $\texttt{max\_samples} = 256$; $\texttt{random\_state} = 42$.

\textbf{Local Outlier Factor (LOF)} computes for each record the ratio of its estimated local reachability density to the mean local reachability density of its $k$-nearest neighbours~\cite{ref25}. Records with LOF $\gg 1.0$ are locally dense isolates. $n_{\text{neighbors}} = 20$; full-training evaluation (\texttt{novelty = False}).

\textbf{Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA)} decomposes input matrix $\mathbf{X}$ into a learned normal representation $\mathbf{L}$ (encoded/decoded by a dense autoencoder) and a sparse noise matrix $\mathbf{S}$ capturing anomalous patterns ($L_1$-penalised)~\cite{ref34}. Anomaly scores are mean squared error (MSE) between the input and reconstruction. Architecture: $[7 \to 32 \to 16 \to 32 \to 7]$, ReLU activations; epochs = 50; batch size = 256; $\lambda_{L1} = 0.01$.

\subsection{Consensus Framework and Typology Mapping}

A record receives \texttt{consensus\_flag = 1} if flagged by $\geq$\,2 of 3 methods. Consensus-flagged records are post-processed through a rule-based typology assignment module mapping feature profiles to seven typology labels (T1--T7). The typology schema synthesises three empirical classification sources: Siregar and Aminudin's modus operandi taxonomy from multi-case East Java analysis~\cite{ref13}, Kartadinata et al.'s systematic classification of 200$+$ KPK prosecution cases~\cite{ref14}, and Hidajat's anti-corruption mode analysis for the Dana Desa context~\cite{ref6}. Table~\ref{tab:typo_rules} presents the full feature-to-typology assignment rules derived from these sources. Records whose combined feature profile satisfies no individual rule threshold receive an \textit{Unclassified} assignment.

\begin{table*}[htb]
\caption{Corruption typology assignment rules for consensus-flagged records. Typology schema derived from triangulation of~\cite{ref6,ref13,ref14}.}
\label{tab:typo_rules}
\begin{tabular*}{\hsize}{@{\extracolsep{\fill}}cll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Code} & \textbf{Typology} & \textbf{Primary Feature Signal} \\
\colrule

\subsection{Village Priority Tier Classification}

Village-level priority tiers aggregate activity-level consensus flags into an anomaly persistence score per village across three fiscal years. Tier~1 (High Priority): consensus flags in $\geq$\,2 years. Tier~2 (Moderate): flags in exactly 1 year. Tier~3 (Not Flagged): no flags. Multi-year persistence operationalises the Opportunity condition in the Fraud Triangle~\cite{ref17}: recurrence signals an entrenched structural vulnerability rather than an incidental deviation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Algorithm Selection: Why LOF's Superiority Is Theoretically Predicted}

LOF's bimodality coefficient of 0.957 is not an empirical coincidence; it is a theoretically predictable consequence of how corruption manifests structurally in Siskeudes activity records. Village fund activities within the same \texttt{Kode\_Output} prefix code form natural local density clusters: road construction activities, PAUD operational expenses, and BLT disbursement entries share characteristic cost ranges, procurement types, and disbursement stage structures. When a fraudulent record occupies such a cluster---for instance, a road construction activity with \texttt{cost\_per\_unit} at 42.42 standard deviations above its category mean---LOF's local density comparison correctly identifies it as a local outlier ($\text{LOF} \gg 1.0$) even when IF's path-partitioning considers it globally unremarkable against the full feature distribution~\cite{ref25}.

IF's unimodal score distribution (BC = 0.335) reflects an architecture misalignment with the fraud structure: village fund corruption clusters systematically by activity type rather than as globally random sparsity, suppressing the score variance that IF requires to produce clean bimodal separation. This does not invalidate IF's detections---it means IF's 7,974 flagged records carry lower individual confidence and higher weight when corroborated by LOF or RDA. The consensus framework resolves this heterogeneity: the IF $\cap$ RDA intersection (50.3\%, $n = 2{,}506$) captures globally extreme anomalies confirmed by two independent global paradigms; the LOF-unique population identifies locally deviant within-category anomalies; the 156 triple-consensus records represent the highest-confidence fraud signals across all three detection modalities. An APIP system relying solely on IF would structurally miss the 3,951 records LOF uniquely identifies---activities whose cost structure is abnormal relative to their specific programme category but unremarkable in the global expenditure distribution.

\subsection{The Fraud Triangle in the Data}

All three Fraud Triangle conditions find quantitative expression in the empirical results. \textit{Pressure} manifests in IF's elevated 2023 anomaly rate (10.5\% versus 6.5--7.1\% in subsequent years), coinciding with post-COVID village budget expansion when disbursement targets created stronger incentives for inflated claims to fully absorb enlarged allocations. \textit{Opportunity} is most directly confirmed by the 98.8\% Swakelola procurement dominance---the structural condition S{\o}reide~\cite{ref9} identifies as the primary enabler of procurement-stage corruption. Self-managed activities face no competitive bidding, no independent price verification, and no external financial audit prior to disbursement. The 642 Tier-1 villages exhibiting multi-year anomaly persistence (47.1\% of villages) demonstrate that Opportunity is not incidental but entrenched: the same villages display irregular patterns across consecutive fiscal years, confirming a stable accountability vacuum.

\textit{Rationalisation} does not produce directly observable quantitative signals but leaves its imprint in the typology distribution. The dominance of T1 (Mark-up, $n = 1{,}571$) and T7 (Cross-Category Dump, $n = 1{,}568$) over operationally complex schemes (T3 Volume Padding = 38; T5 Procurement Irregularity = 26) is consistent with a rationalisation environment where administratively ambiguous schemes---price inflation within defensible deviation ranges, or budget migration across output categories---are perceived as low-detection-risk by perpetrators~\cite{ref17}. The comparative rarity of T2 Ghost Activity ($n = 774$) relative to T1/T7 suggests actors prefer patterns that exploit documentation ambiguity over those requiring systematic falsification of project existence.

\subsection{Principal-Agent Information Asymmetry: Where the Detection Gap Operates}

The dominant RDA error feature---\texttt{avg\_completion}---locates precisely where information asymmetry operates with greatest consequence in the principal-agent relationship~\cite{ref26}. Village heads report T1 disbursement at or near 100\% while T2 and T3 stage realisations approach zero---a tranche progression pattern the autoencoder reconstructed with maximum error (MSE $\approx$ 0.00145) because no legitimate fund absorption behaviour produces this profile. This is not random deviation; it is systematic exploitation of the fact that APIP inspection cycles typically conclude after T3 disbursement windows close, by which time completion reports have already determined subsequent disbursement eligibility.

This finding carries a direct operational implication for APIP inspection design. Currently, most kabupaten-level inspectorates begin examination with procurement documentation (contract comparisons, vendor verification). The RDA error decomposition advises reversing this sequence: cross-checking reported stage completion percentages against field evidence of physical output should be the first analytical step, because the completion reporting channel is where information asymmetry operates most intensively and where document-level manipulation is most feasible without physical corroboration.

\subsection{Subthreshold Masking: Implications for Audit Intelligence}

The 708 unclassified records (22.8\% of consensus flags) warrant explicit analytical attention beyond treating them as a classification residual. These records satisfy the multi-paradigm anomaly threshold---confirmed anomalous by $\geq$\,2 independent methods---yet fail every individual typology rule threshold. The most plausible explanation is compound fraud: schemes combining moderate mark-up with partial completion manipulation across features simultaneously, such that no single feature exceeds its individual typology threshold while the joint feature profile remains statistically anomalous to independent algorithmic perspectives.

For APIP, these records represent the highest-value targets for field investigation precisely because their anomalous nature is not attributable to any single identifiable feature deviation. They cannot be dismissed as data quality artefacts---two independent algorithms confirm their anomalous status---nor pre-classified into a known scheme. An inspector examining a T1-labelled record knows to look for inflated unit prices against procurement documentation; an inspector examining an unclassified record must approach the file with full inter-feature intelligence, cross-referencing completion, cost, and category dimensions simultaneously. These 708 records should be prioritised for in-depth desk review before any field visit assignment.

\subsection{DeLone and McLean IS Success: Evaluating the Pipeline as an IS Artefact}

Applied to this pipeline, the DeLone and McLean model~\cite{ref10} evaluates three quality dimensions. \textit{Information Quality}: consensus anomaly scores, typology labels, and village priority tiers are validated by multi-paradigm convergence, alignment with documented judicial modus operandi~\cite{ref13,ref14}, and the four Jambi prosecution cases confirming that the financial patterns targeted here were present in verified fraud~\cite{ref30,ref31,ref32,ref33}. \textit{Individual Impact}: the ranked priority list converts 99,692 undifferentiated records into a targeted 642-village inspection workplan---a 93.5\% search space reduction that directly addresses the APIP staffing constraint documented by Srirejeki and Faturokhman~\cite{ref12}. \textit{Organisational Impact}: realising this impact requires that APIP inspectorates institutionalise ranked pipeline outputs as formal pre-inspection intelligence inputs---a governance design challenge demanding internal procedure amendments and inspector training on algorithmic output interpretation. The pipeline's IS success therefore hinges not only on technical detection accuracy but on organisational uptake: its information quality dimension is demonstrably satisfied; its individual and organisational impact dimensions remain contingent on institutional adoption decisions.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study develops and evaluates a comparative unsupervised machine learning pipeline for detecting corruption indications in village fund expenditure absorption records, applied to 99,692 activity-level entries collected via jaga.id from Jambi Province across FY~2023--2025. Three research questions were answered empirically.

\textbf{RQ1 --- Feature discriminating power}: Seven engineered features operationalising documented corruption modus operandi~\cite{ref13,ref14} produce a feature matrix with sufficient discriminating power for multi-paradigm anomaly detection without pre-existing fraud labels. RDA reconstruction error diagnosis identifies \texttt{avg\_completion} as the dominant corruption signal (MSE $\approx$ 0.00145), followed by \texttt{cost\_per\_unit} and \texttt{activity\_category}. The extreme right-skew in \texttt{cost\_per\_unit} (max $= 102.83\sigma$) and \texttt{cost\_deviation\_by\_category} (max $= 42.42\sigma$) confirms that price inflation leaves detectable quantitative traces in Siskeudes absorption records without requiring judicial corroboration.

\textbf{RQ2 --- Algorithm performance}: LOF achieves the highest bimodality coefficient (BC = 0.957), demonstrating the sharpest normal-to-anomalous discrimination produced by its local density architecture operating on within-category peer groups. This superiority is theoretically predicted: within-category price inflation is structurally invisible to global methods (IF, BC = 0.335; RDA, BC = 0.703) but directly measurable by LOF's peer-group comparison mechanism. The multi-paradigm consensus framework aggregates complementary detection channels into a 3,107-record high-confidence flagged set that no single algorithm produces independently.

\textbf{RQ3 --- Typology mapping}: Consensus anomalies map predominantly to T1 (Mark-up, 50.6\%) and T7 (Cross-Category Dump, 50.5\%), consistent with Jambi judicial records~\cite{ref30,ref31,ref32,ref33} and prior empirical typology studies~\cite{ref13,ref14}. T2 (Ghost Activity, 24.9\%) ranks third. The 708 unclassified records (22.8\%) represent the methodologically most challenging anomaly class---multi-method confirmed but single-feature subthreshold---requiring inter-feature combined analysis rather than single-indicator examination.

The pipeline operationalises the DeLone and McLean information-quality-to-organisational-impact pathway~\cite{ref10} by reducing APIP's inspection search space from 99,692 records to 642 priority villages (93.5\% reduction) without pre-existing labels. Beyond audit efficiency, the typology-labelled outputs carry direct implications for law enforcement: the 642 Tier-1 priority villages constitute actionable pre-inspection intelligence for APIP inspectorates, enabling resource reallocation to the highest-anomaly-concentration areas. For KPK and Kejaksaan, the typology-labelled anomaly records provide structured documentation of suspicious financial patterns---mark-up amounts, cross-category budget migration profiles, and stage-completion inconsistencies---that can support preliminary inquiry targeting, potentially reducing the two-to-five-year detection lag documented in Jambi prosecution cases~\cite{ref30,ref31,ref32,ref33}.

\textbf{Limitations --- Data}: (1)~Single-province scope limits generalisability to provinces with structurally different disbursement patterns or governance capacity. (2)~jaga.id absorption data is derived from village-entered Siskeudes records; intentional misreporting at data entry constitutes an undetectable systematic data quality risk from the records alone. (3)~Ground-truth labels are unavailable at the activity level; judicial conviction records do not map to specific Siskeudes entries, preventing precision--recall evaluation.

\textbf{Limitations --- Methodology}: (1)~The 5\% contamination rate is an unverified a priori assumption; miscalibration shifts the IF decision boundary into high-density score regions. (2)~Single-threshold typology rules cannot capture compound fraud schemes where multiple features deviate at moderate but jointly anomalous magnitudes. (3)~LOF's $O(n^2)$ neighbourhood computation limits scalability to datasets significantly exceeding 100k records without approximate search optimisation.

\textbf{Future directions}: (1)~Ground-truth label acquisition through systematic matching of pipeline-flagged activities against KPK and Kejaksaan prosecution indictments, building annotated training data to empirically calibrate typology classification accuracy. (2)~Multi-source data fusion integrating SP2D payment transfer records (Kemendagri) with OM-SPAN budget realisation data (Kemenkeu) as cross-validation layers against jaga.id absorption figures, reducing single-source data quality risk. (3)~Adaptive contamination rate calibration using expert-annotated APIP field inspection samples, replacing the fixed 5\% assumption with category-specific empirical estimates. (4)~Temporal extension to the FY~2018--2025 longitudinal panel to improve persistence detection reliability and enable modelling of corruption pattern evolution across dana desa programme maturation phases.
