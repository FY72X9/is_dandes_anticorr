## 1. Introduction

Indonesia's Dana Desa (Village Fund) programme represents one of the largest intergovernmental fiscal transfers in the developing world, channelling approximately Rp 71 trillion annually to 75,259 villages across the archipelago since the enactment of Law No. 6 of 2014 on Villages [1]. The programme's fiscal scale, however, has outpaced the supervisory infrastructure needed to ensure its integrity. By 2024, Indonesian Corruption Watch (ICW) had catalogued 591 court verdicts involving village fund misappropriation, implicating 640 defendants and documenting Rp 598.13 billion in verified state losses [2]. The Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK) identified 851 village fund corruption cases from 2015 onward, in which village heads accounted for more than 60% of perpetrators [3]. These figures converge on a single diagnostic conclusion: the dominant response to village fund fraud remains reactive — prosecution occurring years after diversion — rather than proactive, data-driven detection that operates within disbursement cycles.

The technical infrastructure for proactive detection already exists. The Sistem Keuangan Desa (Siskeudes), maintained by BPKP, records village fund expenditure at the activity level across each disbursement stage: what type of activity was planned, how much budget was allocated, how much was realised per tranche (T1, T2, T3), and through what procurement mechanism. This granular absorption data constitutes an administrative signal archive — one that encodes, in latent form, the financial patterns that distinguish legitimate from corrupt expenditure. Yet no systematic, automated screening mechanism currently operates at the district or provincial level to interrogate this archive before inspection cycles conclude and disbursement windows close.

Existing research on dana desa fraud concentrates overwhelmingly on retrospective forensic analysis [4], governance accountability frameworks [5], and fraud triangle diagnostics [6]. Where machine learning enters the literature, it applies predominantly supervised classification — a paradigm that requires labelled ground truth unavailable in real-time monitoring contexts [13, 14]. The present study addresses this gap directly: it develops an unsupervised learning pipeline that detects expenditure anomalies in Jambi Province's village fund expenditure records across fiscal years 2023–2025, without presupposing any pre-existing fraud labels.

**Jambi Province provides a structurally significant research site.** The jaga.id civil-society transparency platform recorded only 11 complaint submissions attributed to Jambi Province — 1.4% of 761 national reports — while provinces of comparable administrative scale, such as Sumatera Utara (81 complaints) and Sumatera Selatan (48 complaints), registered substantially higher reporting volumes [27]. Low voluntary reporting does not signal low fraud exposure; it signals a low-monitoring environment. The jaga.id platform, operated by KPK as a public transparency infrastructure for village fund accountability, additionally provides open-access village fund expenditure absorption data at the activity level; these records constitute the primary dataset from which this study's longitudinal panel was constructed [27]. Alfada's panel GMM analysis across 19 Indonesian provinces confirms that regions combining high intergovernmental transfer dependence with weak accountability structures exhibit systematically elevated corruption incidence — the structural profile directly applicable to Jambi [29]. The inspectorate capacity gap is equally documented: Srirejeki and Faturokhman [12] show that kabupaten-level APIP offices lack operational staffing to screen thousands of individual village activity records per disbursement cycle, producing a measurable shortfall between the monitoring mandate under Government Regulation No. 60/2008 and actual inspection coverage.

Four contemporaneous prosecution cases in Jambi Province — collectively documenting Rp 2.301 billion in verified losses across Kerinci, Muaro Jambi, and Tanjung Jabung Timur kabupaten [30, 31, 32, 33] — confirm that the detection lag between fund diversion and prosecution ranges from two to five years. All four cases involved financial irregularities that generated quantifiable signals in Siskeudes absorption records: fictitious construction outputs, zero-realisation procurement activities, and cross-period fund diversion — precisely the patterns the engineered features in this study target.

This study asks three questions: (1) Which feature constructs derived from village fund expenditure absorption data serve as the most discriminating signals of expenditure anomaly? (2) Which algorithm — Isolation Forest (IF), Local Outlier Factor (LOF), or Robust Deep Autoencoder (RDA) — demonstrates superior anomaly discrimination on Jambi village fund data? (3) How do algorithmically identified anomalies map to established corruption typologies documented in judicial and institutional records?

The remainder of this paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on corruption typology, anomaly detection methodology, and Indonesia-specific dana desa fraud literature. Section 3 describes the dataset, feature engineering protocol, and three-algorithm pipeline. Section 4 presents empirical results. Section 5 interprets findings through the Fraud Triangle, Principal-Agent Theory, and the DeLone and McLean IS Success Model. Section 6 concludes with implications for APIP inspection practice and future research directions.
