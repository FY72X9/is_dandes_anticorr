## A. Corruption Typology & Classification

[1] J. Bussell, "Typologies of corruption: a pragmatic approach," in *Greed, Corruption, and the Modern State*, Edward Elgar Publishing, 2015. DOI: [10.4337/9781784714703.00007](https://doi.org/10.4337/9781784714703.00007)
- **Abstract**: Develops a pragmatic two-dimensional typology distinguishing corruption by *access* (money vs. preference) and by *level* (bureaucratic vs. political). Widely cited framework for operationalising corruption types in empirical research.
- **Relevance**: Primary typology framework — directly informs classification of dana desa corruption modus by access type.
- **OA PDF**: https://www.elgaronline.com/downloadpdf/edcoll/9781784714697/9781784714697.00007.pdf
- **Citations**: 63

[2] J. G. Vargas-Hernández, "The multiple faces of corruption: typology, forms and levels," *SSRN Electronic Journal*, 2009. DOI: [10.2139/ssrn.1413976](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.1413976)
- **Abstract**: Synthesises corruption typologies across political, economic, administrative, and sociocultural dimensions. Distinguishes between grand, petty, and institutional corruption and maps them to structural conditions.
- **Relevance**: Multi-dimensional typology covering forms (bribery, embezzlement, fraud, nepotism) applicable to village-level public financial management.
- **OA URL**: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.1413976
- **Citations**: 54

[3] N. Suleiman and Z. Othman, "Corruption typology: a review of literature," *Contemporary Business Review*, vol. 16, no. 2, pp. 102–108, 2017.
- **Abstract**: Systematic literature review mapping corruption typologies from 1970 to 2016. Identifies convergence around functional types (bribery, extortion, fraud, embezzlement, favouritism) and emerging hybrid categories.
- **Relevance**: Comprehensive typology review — valuable for building Corruption Typology Mapping table in the research.
- **OA PDF**: http://repo.uum.edu.my/26265/1/CBR%2016%202%202017%20102%20108.pdf
- **Citations**: 20

[4] J. S. Albanese and K. Artello, "The behavior of corruption: an empirical typology of public corruption by objective and method," *Actual Problems of Economics and Law*, vol. 13, no. 2, pp. 1215–1229, 2019. DOI: [10.21202/1993-047x.13.2019.2.1215-1229](https://doi.org/10.21202/1993-047x.13.2019.2.1215-1229)
- **Abstract**: Constructs an empirical typology of public corruption by classifying 500+ US federal cases along two axes: corruption *objective* (financial gain, preferential treatment, avoiding loss) and *method* (bribery, fraud, conflict of interest). Provides evidence-based classification system grounded in judicial records.
- **Relevance**: Empirical typology method directly analogous to modus operandi classification from KPK/ICW judicial verdicts.
- **OA PDF**: http://apel.ieml.ru/storage/archive_articles/9910.pdf
- **Citations**: 13

[5] F. Mutungi, R. Baguma, A. H. Ejiri, and T. Janowski, "Digital anti-corruption typology for public service delivery," *International Journal of Computer Applications*, vol. 183, no. 5, 2021. DOI: [10.5120/ijca2021921089](https://doi.org/10.5120/ijca2021921089)
- **Abstract**: Develops a digital anti-corruption typology specifically for IS-mediated public services. Classifies corruption behaviours by digital interaction point (input, processing, output) and maps IS-based countermeasures to each type.
- **Relevance**: Directly situates corruption typology within the IS domain — rare intersection of IS and anti-corruption classification relevant to the research's IS framing.
- **OA URL**: https://doi.org/10.5120/ijca2021921089
- **Citations**: 6

[6] M. Sommersguter-Reichmann, C. Wild, A. Stepan, G. Reichmann, and A. Fried, "Individual and institutional corruption in European and US healthcare: overview and link of various corruption typologies," *Applied Health Economics and Health Policy*, vol. 16, no. 3, pp. 261–278, 2018. DOI: [10.1007/s40258-018-0386-6](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40258-018-0386-6)
- **Abstract**: Provides a cross-domain corruption typology linking individual-level (bribery, kickback, embezzlement) and institutional-level (conflict of interest, regulatory capture) forms to sector-specific vulnerabilities. Uses healthcare as an analogy for public service delivery systems.
- **Relevance**: Cross-sector typology useful for analogical reasoning — healthcare service delivery corruption patterns parallel village fund expenditure categories.
- **OA PDF**: https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007%2Fs40258-018-0386-6.pdf
- **Citations**: 48

[7] A. Graycar, "Corruption: classification and analysis," *Policy and Society*, vol. 34, no. 2, pp. 87–96, 2015. DOI: [10.1016/j.polsoc.2015.04.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.polsoc.2015.04.001)
- **Abstract**: Develops the TASP framework (Types, Activities, Sectors, Places) for systematic classification of corruption. Demonstrates that identifying *what type* of corruption occurs, *in what activity*, *in which sector*, and *in which place* is an essential precondition for designing targeted anti-corruption controls. Empirically validated with corruption data from New York City/State and Victoria, Australia.
- **Relevance**: TASP classification directly supports Research Question 3 — mapping algorithmically detected anomalies to specific corruption typologies (mark-up, fictitious projects, procurement irregularities) by activity type and sector context.
- **OA PDF**: https://academic.oup.com/policyandsociety/article-pdf/34/2/87/42581208/j.polsoc.2015.04.001.pdf
- **Citations**: 167

## B. Corruption: Causes, Consequences, and Public Procurement

[8] V. Tanzi, "Corruption around the world: causes, consequences, scope, and cures," *IMF Staff Papers*, vol. 45, no. 4, pp. 559–594, 1998. DOI: [10.2307/3867585](https://doi.org/10.2307/3867585) (published). SSRN preprint: [10.2139/ssrn.882334](https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.882334).
- **Abstract**: Macro-level taxonomy of corruption distinguishing by payer (demand-side vs. supply-side), sector (public procurement, tax, regulation), and effect on economic outcomes. Foundational framework for measuring corruption scope in public financial management.
- **Relevance**: Foundational conceptual basis for classifying government expenditure corruption — directly applicable to dana desa mark-up and procurement irregularities.
- **OA URL**: https://doi.org/10.2139/ssrn.882334
- **Citations**: 420

[9] T. Søreide, "Corruption in public procurement: causes, consequences and cures," *CMI Report R 2002:1*, Chr. Michelsen Institute, Bergen, Norway, 2002.
- **Abstract**: Analyses corruption specifically in public procurement chains — bidding, contracting, invoicing, and delivery stages. Distinguishes between collusive, coercive, and extortive forms of procurement corruption.
- **Relevance**: Directly maps to *Cara Pengadaan* variable in the dataset — swakelola vs. contracted procurement corruption typology.
- **OA URL**: http://hdl.handle.net/11250/2435744 (repository handle — navigate to page for PDF download)
- **Citations**: 129

[10] Š. Šumah, "Corruption, causes and consequences," in *Trade and Global Market*, InTechOpen, 2018. DOI: [10.5772/intechopen.72953](https://doi.org/10.5772/intechopen.72953)
- **Abstract**: Synthesises cross-disciplinary literature on corruption causes (weak institutions, low salaries, culture) and consequences (reduced investment, degraded public service quality, institutional erosion). Provides structured causal model applicable to developing-country contexts.
- **Relevance**: Provides theoretical grounding for why village-level corruption emerges under specific structural conditions in Indonesia.
- **OA PDF**: https://www.intechopen.com/citation-pdf-url/58969
- **Citations**: 70

## C. Village Fund (Dana Desa) Corruption — Indonesia

[11] A. Triyono, "Framing analysis of village funding corruption in media Suaramerdeka.com in Central Java, Indonesia, 2019," *International Journal of Criminology and Sociology*, vol. 9, pp. 1292–1301, 2020. DOI: [10.6000/1929-4409.2020.09.136](https://doi.org/10.6000/1929-4409.2020.09.136)
- **Abstract**: Analyses media framing of dana desa corruption cases in Central Java, identifying dominant corruption narratives (mark-up, misappropriation, fictitious programmes) and the typological patterns represented in media coverage of KPK prosecutions.
- **Relevance**: Documents real modus operandi types from Indonesian court records, providing narrative basis for corruption typology mapping.
- **OA PDF**: https://lifescienceglobal.com/pms/index.php/ijcs/article/download/7830/4098
- **Citations**: 6

[12] K. Srirejeki and A. Faturokhman, "In search of corruption prevention model: case study from Indonesia village fund," *Acta Universitatis Danubius. Oeconomica*, vol. 16, no. 3, pp. 214–229, 2020.
- **Abstract**: Case study examining corruption patterns in Indonesia's village fund programme. Identifies three dominant prevention gaps: weak internal controls, inadequate community oversight, and limited auditor capacity at kabupaten level.
- **Relevance**: Governance analysis directly contextualising why unsupervised ML detection is needed — existing preventive mechanisms are insufficient.
- **OA URL**: https://doaj.org/article/01b1588b59f149ba82d6ef47dddaca0a
- **Citations**: 5

[13] R. K. Siregar and A. Aminudin, "Abuse of village fund (VF) in Indonesia: case study of VF corruption in East Java," *PEOPLE: International Journal of Social Sciences*, vol. 6, no. 1, pp. 379–396, 2020. DOI: [10.20319/pijss.2020.61.379396](https://doi.org/10.20319/pijss.2020.61.379396)
- **Abstract**: Multi-case analysis of village fund corruption in East Java, classifying modus operandi into five types: mark-up of goods/services, fictitious budget items, double budgeting, procurement manipulation, and misuse of personnel funds. Correlates corruption types to village head characteristics and fund allocation size.
- **Relevance**: Most directly applicable typology study — five modus operandi types align with feature engineering targets in the research.
- **OA PDF**: https://content.openalex.org/works/W3020760472.pdf (OpenAlex content store — confirmed available)
- **OA PDF (original, broken)**: https://grdspublishing.org/index.php/people/article/download/2301/3751

[14] A. Kartadinata, M. Ghifari, and F. Santiago, "Criminal policy of village fund corruption in Indonesia," in *Proc. 1st Int. Conf. on Law, Social Science, Economics, and Education (ICLSSEE 2021)*, Jakarta, Indonesia, 2021. DOI: [10.4108/eai.6-3-2021.2306470](https://doi.org/10.4108/eai.6-3-2021.2306470)
- **Abstract**: Reviews criminal law policy responses to village fund corruption in Indonesia, analysing 200+ KPK prosecution cases (2015–2020). Identifies dominant perpetrator profiles (village heads, village secretaries) and recurring modus: penggelembungan (inflated costs), proyek fiktif (fictitious projects), nepotism in procurement.

## D. Machine Learning for Fraud and Anomaly Detection

[16] E. Stripling, B. Baesens, B. Chizi, and S. vanden Broucke, "Isolation-based conditional anomaly detection on mixed-attribute data to uncover workers' compensation fraud," *Decision Support Systems*, vol. 111, pp. 13–26, 2018. DOI: [10.1016/j.dss.2018.04.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.dss.2018.04.001)
- **Abstract**: Applies Isolation Forest-based conditional anomaly detection to structured, mixed-attribute government claims data. Demonstrates that Isolation Forest outperforms Mahalanobis distance and LOF methods when attribute interactions encode the fraud signal. Evaluates using domain expert validation over unlabelled data.
- **Relevance**: Direct methodological precedent — unlabelled government financial data, similar feature structure to dana desa expenditure records, and expert-validated evaluation protocol.
- **OA URL**: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S016792361830068X

## E. Theoretical Foundations

[17] D. R. Cressey, *Other People's Money: A Study in the Social Psychology of Embezzlement*. Glencoe, IL: Free Press, 1953.
- **Abstract**: Original formulation of the Fraud Triangle, establishing three necessary conditions for occupational fraud: perceived unshareable financial problem (Pressure), perceived opportunity (Opportunity), and verbal justification (Rationalisation). Derived from interviews with 133 convicted embezzlers.
- **Relevance**: Foundational theoretical framework — Fraud Triangle underpins the theoretical basis for why anomalous expenditure patterns constitute fraud signals.
- **OA PDF**: Not available (pre-digital monograph). Widely available through institutional library access.

[18] W. H. DeLone and E. R. McLean, "The DeLone and McLean model of information systems success: a ten-year update," *Journal of Management Information Systems*, vol. 19, no. 4, pp. 9–30, 2003. DOI: [10.1080/07421222.2003.11045748](https://doi.org/10.1080/07421222.2003.11045748)
- **Abstract**: Ten-year update and extension of the D&M IS Success Model, incorporating *service quality* as a sixth dimension. The model links information quality → system quality → service quality → use/user satisfaction → individual impact → organisational impact.
- **Relevance**: IS theoretical framing of the anomaly detection system as an IS artefact — its contribution is measured by organisational impact (corruption deterrence), not technical metrics alone.
- **OA PDF**: Not openly available; accessible via Taylor & Francis institutional subscription.

[19] F. T. Liu, K. M. Ting, and Z.-H. Zhou, "Isolation forest," in *Proc. 8th IEEE Int. Conf. on Data Mining (ICDM 2008)*, Pisa, Italy, pp. 413–422, 2008. DOI: [10.1109/ICDM.2008.17](https://doi.org/10.1109/ICDM.2008.17)
- **Abstract**: Introduces Isolation Forest — an ensemble anomaly detection algorithm exploiting the isolation property: anomalies are isolated in fewer partitioning steps than normal observations. Demonstrates linear time complexity O(n) and superior performance over LOF and random forests on standard benchmarks including large-scale datasets.
- **Relevance**: Primary algorithmic method in the comparative study — provides the foundational theoretical justification for using path-length as anomaly score proxy.
- **OA URL**: Available via IEEE Xplore preview + ResearchGate author copy. DOI landing: https://doi.org/10.1109/ICDM.2008.17

[20] F. T. Liu, K. M. Ting, and Z.-H. Zhou, "Isolation-based anomaly detection," *ACM Transactions on Knowledge Discovery from Data*, vol. 6, no. 1, article 3, 2012. DOI: [10.1145/2133360.2133363](https://doi.org/10.1145/2133360.2133363)
- **Abstract**: Extended journal version of the ICDM 2008 paper. Formalises the isolation mechanism, proves theoretical properties of the iForest estimator, and introduces mass-based dissimilarity as a generalisation. Extends experiments to real-world datasets including financial anomaly benchmarks.
- **Relevance**: Journal-quality formal treatment of Isolation Forest theory — provides citation authority for the method beyond the conference paper.
- **OA URL**: ACM Digital Library (author version available). DOI: https://doi.org/10.1145/2133360.2133363

## B-ext. Corruption Causes & Theoretical Reviews (Extended)

[21] J. Svensson, "Eight questions about corruption," *Journal of Economic Perspectives*, vol. 19, no. 3, pp. 19–42, 2005. DOI: [10.1257/089533005774357860](https://doi.org/10.1257/089533005774357860)
- **Abstract**: Synthesises empirical evidence and theory on corruption by examining eight key questions: how it is measured, what causes it, why it persists, and what actually reduces it. Distinguishes between corruption incentivised by *access* (regulatory rents) and that driven by *opportunity* (weak monitoring). Reviews cross-country evidence on the relationship between GDP, rule of law, and corruption levels.
- **Relevance**: Canonical review article (1,697 citations) establishing the macro-theoretical rationale for why weak monitoring structures in Indonesia's village governance produce systematic expenditure irregularities — directly justifying the unsupervised detection approach.
- **OA URL**: World Bank preprint available at https://documents.worldbank.org/curated/en/486981468762385907. JEP article: https://doi.org/10.1257/089533005774357860
- **Citations**: 1,697

## C-ext. Village Fund Corruption — Indonesia (Extended)

[22] T. Hidajat, "Village fund corruption mode: an anti-corruption perspective in Indonesia," *Journal of Financial Crime*, vol. 31, no. 6, pp. 1454–1467, 2024. DOI: [10.1108/jfc-01-2024-0042](https://doi.org/10.1108/jfc-01-2024-0042)
- **Abstract**: Applies Diamond Fraud Theory to explain persistence of village fund corruption in Indonesia. Identifies five recurring corruption modes: fund misuse, cover-up schemes, fictional financial reports, fictitious project activities, and budget mark-up. Argues prevention and detection are more effective when all four Diamond Fraud elements (pressure, opportunity, rationalisation, capability) are addressed simultaneously.
- **Relevance**: Highest-impact academic source (Emerald/Scopus, *Journal of Financial Crime*) to directly analyse dana desa corruption modus using an established fraud theory — validates the research's five-mode classification schema.
- **OA URL**: Not openly available. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1108/jfc-01-2024-0042 (Emerald institutional access)
- **Citations**: 4

[23] A. Alfada, "Does fiscal decentralization encourage corruption in local governments? Evidence from Indonesia," *Journal of Risk and Financial Management*, vol. 12, no. 3, article 118, 2019. DOI: [10.3390/jrfm12030118](https://doi.org/10.3390/jrfm12030118)
- **Abstract**: Uses panel data GMM estimator for 19 Indonesian provinces (2004–2014) to assess whether fiscal decentralisation drives or mitigates local government corruption. Finds that both expenditure and tax-revenue decentralisation increase corruption cases measured by KPK prosecution data. Identifies weak accountability, low human capital, and intergovernmental grant dependency as aggravating factors.
- **Relevance**: Empirical evidence that fiscal decentralisation — the structural mechanism enabling dana desa — systematically increases corruption risk at the local level, contextualising why the Jambi province dataset exhibits detectable anomalies.
- **OA PDF**: https://www.mdpi.com/1911-8074/12/3/118/pdf?version=1563097208
- **Citations**: 45

## D-ext. ML & Anomaly Detection (Extended)

[24] M. Ester, H.-P. Kriegel, J. Sander, and X. Xu, "A density-based algorithm for discovering clusters in large spatial databases with noise," in *Proc. 2nd Int. Conf. on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining (KDD-96)*, AAAI Press, Portland, OR, 1996, pp. 226–231.
- **Abstract**: Introduces DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise), classifying points as core points, border points, or noise by density-reachability thresholds (ε, MinPts). Anomalous records emerge as noise points — isolated data objects that fall outside any dense cluster — making DBSCAN inherently dual-purpose: it discovers clusters and flags outliers simultaneously without labelled training data.
- **Relevance**: Primary algorithmic source for DBSCAN as the second method in the three-method comparative study. Dana desa expenditure records deviating in cost, volume, and procurement pattern become the noise points that DBSCAN naturally isolates.
- **OA URL**: ACM DL: https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/3001460.3001507 · CiteSeerX: http://www2.cs.uh.edu/~ceick/7363/Papers/dbscan.pdf
- **Citations**: 19,132

[25] Y.-B. Kim and M. A. Vasarhelyi, "Anomaly detection with the density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (DBSCAN) to detect potentially fraudulent wire transfers," *International Journal of Digital Accounting Research*, vol. 24, 2024. DOI: [10.4192/1577-8517-v24_3](https://doi.org/10.4192/1577-8517-v24_3)
- **Abstract**: Applies DBSCAN to structured financial transaction data (wire transfers) from a US financial institution to identify anomalous records without labelled training data. Demonstrates DBSCAN's practical applicability in financial fraud detection by tuning ε and MinPts parameters on real transaction records; anomalous transactions emerge as noise points with high audit investigation priority.
- **Relevance**: Direct methodological precedent for DBSCAN applied to structured government financial data — analogous to dana desa expenditure records — and establishes the validation protocol using expert auditor review, paralleling the proposed expert-validated evaluation in the research.
- **OA PDF**: http://dx.doi.org/10.4192/1577-8517-v24_3 (bronze OA)
- **Citations**: 1

## E-ext. Theoretical Foundations (Extended)

[26] N. Groenendijk, "A principal-agent model of corruption," *Crime, Law and Social Change*, vol. 27, no. 3–4, pp. 207–229, 1997. DOI: [10.1023/A:1008267601329](https://doi.org/10.1023/A:1008267601329)
- **Abstract**: Develops a three-tier principal-agent-client model of corruption, formalising conditions under which agents (bureaucrats/officials) exploit information asymmetries to engage in corruption. Demonstrates that corruption probability decreases when monitoring intensity increases and when institutional incentive structures close the opportunity gap between principals and agents.
- **Relevance**: Foundational formal model for the principal-agent mechanism applied throughout the dana desa governance chain. Village heads (agents) exploit information asymmetries relative to kabupaten monitoring authorities (principals), the exact condition analysed in several sources in `Referensi_laporan_korupsi.md`.
- **OA PDF**: University of Twente repository: https://ris.utwente.nl/ws/files/6653794/principal-agent.pdf
- **Citations**: 157
